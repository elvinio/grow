# How Learning Works

Most people spend years in school without ever being taught how learning actually happens. We sit in class, read notes, maybe highlight a few things — and hope it sticks. For many Singapore students, this pattern continues straight through to the A-Level examinations and beyond, propped up by sheer repetition and hours of tuition.

The problem is not effort. Most Singapore students work incredibly hard. The problem is that the methods many of us rely on — re-reading, copying notes, watching YouTube explanations passively — are among the least effective ways to get information to stick in long-term memory. Understanding why requires a brief look inside the brain.

---

## What Happens in the Brain During Learning

Learning is not like downloading a file. It is an active biological process that involves encoding, storage, and retrieval — three distinct stages that each require different conditions to work well.

**Encoding** is the first step: taking information from the outside world and converting it into a neural signal. This happens when you read, listen, observe, or do something. But encoding alone does not mean learning. Encoding just means the information entered your sensory system. Whether it goes further depends on attention and meaning.

**Storage** is what people typically mean when they say "learning." Information moves from working memory into long-term memory through a process that strengthens synaptic connections — the links between neurons. The more connections a piece of information has to existing knowledge, the more robustly it is stored. This is why new concepts are easier to learn when tied to concrete examples or personal experience.

**Retrieval** is the most underestimated stage. Every time you successfully recall a piece of information, you strengthen the neural pathways associated with it. Memory is not like a video recording you simply play back — it is reconstructive, and the act of retrieving something actually makes it more memorable in future. This is the foundation of what researchers call the "testing effect," which is explored in detail in [Memory & Retention](./memory-and-retention.md).

## Cognitive Load Theory: Working Memory Has Limits

Your working memory — the mental space you use to process information in the moment — is severely limited. Research by cognitive psychologist George Miller in the 1950s suggested it holds around 7 items at once; more recent work suggests the number may be closer to 4 meaningful "chunks." Either way, it is a small space.

Cognitive Load Theory, developed by educational psychologist John Sweller in the 1980s, explains why this matters enormously for learning. When working memory is overwhelmed — when too much new information arrives at once, or when instructions are confusing, or when a task requires holding too many things in mind simultaneously — learning breaks down. Students may be physically present and nominally paying attention, but nothing is being encoded effectively.

There are three types of cognitive load to be aware of:

- **Intrinsic load:** The inherent difficulty of the material itself. Some things are just harder than others.
- **Extraneous load:** Load created by poor presentation — confusing explanations, cluttered slides, unnecessary distractions. This is waste. Good teaching minimises it.
- **Germane load:** The mental effort involved in forming schemas — organising knowledge into meaningful structures. This is the useful kind of cognitive load.

**What this means practically:**
- Teach or study one idea at a time before adding complexity.
- Remove distractions. Having a phone nearby — even face-down — measurably reduces available cognitive capacity.
- Chunking material into organised structures (mind maps, outlines, numbered steps) reduces intrinsic load and frees up working memory.
- For beginners, worked examples reduce extraneous load better than open-ended problem-solving. Save the exploration for once a foundation is established.

## Sleep: The Brain's Filing System

This is perhaps the most underrated part of the learning process, and the one most ruthlessly sacrificed during exam season in Singapore.

During sleep — particularly during slow-wave sleep and REM sleep — the brain consolidates memories. It replays experiences from the day, strengthens the connections that matter, and prunes away weaker or less relevant signals. This is not a passive process. It is active memory consolidation.

Research by neuroscientist Matthew Walker and others has shown that sleep deprivation impairs the hippocampus — the brain region critical for forming new memories — by up to 40%. That means a student who studies late into the night and sleeps five hours is likely retaining less than 60% of what a well-rested student would.

The implication is counterintuitive: a student who studies for two hours and sleeps eight hours may outperform one who studies for five hours and sleeps four. Sleep is not a reward for finishing studying. It is part of the study process itself.

**Practical guidance:**
- Prioritise 8–9 hours of sleep for primary and secondary school students.
- Study the most important material in the hour or two before sleep — consolidation is strongest for recently encoded information.
- Avoid screen time in the 30–60 minutes before sleep; blue light suppresses melatonin and delays sleep onset.
- Even a 20-minute nap in the afternoon has been shown to improve retention of material studied before the nap.

## Deliberate Practice vs Passive Exposure

Not all practice is equal. Psychologist Anders Ericsson spent decades studying expert performance and identified what separates people who improve rapidly from those who plateau. He called it deliberate practice.

Deliberate practice has several key features:
- It targets specific weaknesses, not areas of comfort.
- It involves immediate, specific feedback.
- It operates at the edge of current ability — slightly beyond the comfortable zone.
- It requires focused attention; it cannot be done on autopilot.

Compare this to passive exposure: re-reading notes, listening to a teacher without engaging, watching a tutor solve problems without attempting them first. These feel productive — they are comfortable, low-effort, and give a false sense of familiarity. Researchers call this "fluency illusion": the material feels familiar, so we mistake familiarity for mastery.

True mastery requires generating, not just recognising. Writing an explanation in your own words. Solving a problem from scratch. Teaching the concept to someone else. These are all forms of deliberate practice.

## Why Re-Reading Feels Productive But Isn't

Re-reading is the default study strategy for most Singapore students. It is also one of the least effective techniques according to cognitive science research.

In a landmark 2013 review of ten common study strategies by Dunlosky and colleagues, re-reading was rated as having "low utility." The reason is simple: re-reading creates familiarity, not retrieval strength. When you see something a second or third time, your brain recognises it — and that recognition is mistaken for knowing. But recognition is not the same as being able to produce the answer on demand in an exam, or apply the concept to a new problem.

The same applies to highlighting and underlining. These are passive activities that feel constructive but require almost no cognitive engagement. A page of yellow highlighter does not equal a page of learning.

What actually works — retrieval practice, spaced repetition, elaborative interrogation — is covered in detail in [Memory & Retention](./memory-and-retention.md) and [Learning Strategies](./learning-strategies.md).

## The Singapore Angle: When Exam Culture Works Against the Brain

Singapore's education system has many genuine strengths — it is coherent, well-resourced, and produces internationally competitive outcomes. But its high-stakes exam structure creates several conditions that actively work against how brains learn best.

**The cramming trap.** Because examinations happen at fixed points in time, students — and parents — tend to compress studying into the weeks immediately before. But spaced practice over months vastly outperforms massed practice (cramming) in terms of long-term retention. The irony is that for the exam itself, cramming can work — information is fresh enough. But six months later, almost none of it survives.

**The marks-for-methods problem.** Many Singapore assessments reward specific phrasings and mark-scheme language, which encourages students to memorise model answers rather than develop deep understanding. This trains pattern recognition, not flexible thinking — which is increasingly what universities and employers actually need.

**Sleep sacrifice.** Study culture in many Singapore households involves late nights, early mornings, and packed schedules with tuition, CCAs, and homework. The resulting chronic sleep debt directly undermines the consolidation process that makes all that studying worthwhile.

**The tuition paradox.** Singapore has one of the highest rates of private tuition in the world. Tuition is not inherently bad — good tuition provides targeted feedback and structured practice. But tuition that simply re-exposes students to content (re-teaching, re-reading) without requiring active retrieval is unlikely to add much. When evaluating a tutor or tuition centre, ask: how much of the session involves students actively retrieving and applying knowledge, versus listening?

None of this means the system is broken or that effort is wasted. It means that smart, evidence-informed choices about *how* to study — not just *how much* — can make a substantial difference within the system as it exists.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Learning involves encoding, storage, and retrieval — all three matter, but retrieval is the most neglected.
- Working memory is limited; reduce extraneous cognitive load to help information reach long-term memory.
- Sleep is not optional — it is when memory consolidation actually happens.
- Deliberate practice at the edge of ability beats passive re-exposure every time.
- Re-reading creates familiarity, not mastery; resist the comfort of passive review.
- Singapore's exam culture, while effective in some ways, creates structural incentives that misalign with how brains actually retain knowledge.
