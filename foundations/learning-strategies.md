# Learning Strategies

If you only read one file in this knowledge base, make it this one. The research on effective learning strategies is unusually clear — we know, with high confidence, which techniques produce durable learning and which ones waste time. The gap between what most students do and what the evidence recommends is wide.

This file gives you a practical, evidence-based toolkit and a quick-reference summary you can return to whenever you need it.

---

## The 6 Evidence-Based Strategies from Cognitive Science

In 2013, researchers John Dunlosky and colleagues published a comprehensive review of ten common study techniques, rating each on utility based on the depth and consistency of evidence supporting them. Two strategies received the highest rating. Others fared poorly. Here is what the evidence says.

### 1. Retrieval Practice (Active Recall)

**What it is:** Actively pulling information from memory, without looking at your notes. Flashcards, practice tests, writing down everything you remember on a blank page, answering questions from memory.

**Why it works:** Every time you successfully retrieve a memory, the neural pathway associated with it is strengthened. The brain treats retrieved information as worth keeping. This is called the "testing effect" — and it holds even when you get the answer wrong, as long as you then check the correct answer.

**How to do it:**
- After reading a section, close the book and write down the key points from memory.
- Use flashcards with a question on one side and the answer on the other.
- Answer past-year exam questions without first re-reading the relevant notes.
- Use the "brain dump" technique: at the start of each study session, spend five minutes writing everything you can recall about the topic.

---

### 2. Spaced Practice (Distributed Practice)

**What it is:** Spreading study of a topic across multiple sessions over time, rather than massing all study into one block.

**Why it works:** Each time you return to a topic after a gap, your brain has partially forgotten it — and the act of re-learning strengthens the memory more than if you had never forgotten it. Forgetting a little and then retrieving it is more powerful than never forgetting at all.

**How to do it:**
- Build a revision calendar that revisits each topic multiple times, with increasing intervals (see [Memory & Retention](./memory-and-retention.md) for a full schedule).
- Use Anki or a manual spaced repetition schedule.
- Never study a topic only once; always plan at least two or three revisits.

---

### 3. Interleaving

**What it is:** Mixing different topics or problem types within a single study session, rather than completing all problems of one type before moving to the next.

**Why it works:** Interleaving forces the brain to actively choose the right strategy or schema for each new problem, rather than running on the momentum of the previous problem. This builds flexible, transferable knowledge — the kind that works in exams where you cannot predict what order topics will appear.

**How to do it:**
- Instead of completing an entire problem set on one topic, alternate between two or three topics.
- Use mixed-topic practice papers and past-year exams.
- After studying Algebra and Geometry separately, do a session with mixed questions from both.

**Important caveat:** Interleaving works best once you have a basic grasp of each individual topic. Do not interleave when you are still completely lost on one or more topics — build foundation first.

---

### 4. Elaborative Interrogation

**What it is:** Asking "why?" and "how?" as you study — generating explanations for why facts are true or why relationships exist.

**Why it works:** Forcing yourself to explain *why* something is true creates connections between new information and existing knowledge. Information stored with rich connections is far easier to retrieve than isolated facts.

**How to do it:**
- As you read, stop regularly and ask: "Why is this true? How does this connect to what I already know?"
- Before accepting a fact, try to explain the mechanism behind it.
- In Science: "Why does this reaction happen?" In History: "Why did this event lead to that outcome?" In Maths: "Why does this formula work?"

---

### 5. Concrete Examples

**What it is:** Grounding abstract concepts in specific, concrete instances.

**Why it works:** Abstract concepts are harder to remember and apply than concrete ones. Concrete examples activate different parts of the brain and give you mental "anchors" — when you encounter the abstract concept again, the concrete example helps you retrieve its meaning.

**How to do it:**
- When you learn a new concept, immediately generate at least two personal examples.
- When a teacher explains an abstract rule, ask yourself: "What does this look like in real life?"
- When helping a student, explain through story and example before definition.

---

### 6. Dual Coding

**What it is:** Combining verbal information with visual representations — diagrams, timelines, concept maps, sketches.

**Why it works:** The brain processes verbal and visual information through distinct but connected systems. Encoding information in both formats creates a richer, more redundant memory trace. Dual coding is particularly effective for complex systems, processes, and causal relationships.

**How to do it:**
- Sketch a diagram from memory after reading a verbal explanation.
- Create concept maps linking key ideas visually.
- Use timelines for historical sequences.
- Draw the problem before solving it mathematically.

Note: the visual does not need to be beautiful. A rough sketch done in 60 seconds is as effective as a carefully drawn one — it is the mental translation process that creates value.

## What to Stop Doing

The same Dunlosky review rated the following techniques as having **low utility** — meaning they are time-consuming relative to their benefit:

| Technique | Why it feels productive | Why it isn't |
|---|---|---|
| Re-reading | Creates familiarity with the material | Familiarity is not the same as retrieval strength |
| Highlighting | Feels active; marks what seems important | Almost no cognitive processing required |
| Summarising | Writing engages the brain somewhat | Effect is weak unless combined with active recall |
| Keyword mnemonics | Can help with paired-associate learning | Limited value beyond specific vocab tasks |

This does not mean these techniques are useless for all purposes. Summarising a complex text can help you understand it initially. Highlighting can be a prereading tool. But using these as your *primary* revision strategies is a poor use of time.

## How to Build a Personal Study System

An effective study system is not a rigid routine — it is a set of principles applied flexibly to your own schedule, subjects, and learning patterns.

**Step 1: Know your current weak points.**
Before each revision period, do a diagnostic scan. Attempt practice questions across all topics without notes. The results tell you where to invest your time. Do not study what you already know — study what you do not.

**Step 2: Schedule revisits, not just initial study.**
For each topic you cover, immediately schedule the next two or three review sessions on your calendar. Without planned revisits, the forgetting curve will do its work.

**Step 3: Make retrieval your default activity.**
Whenever you sit down to revise, your first activity should be retrieval — writing down what you know, answering questions, testing yourself. Notes are for checking, not reading.

**Step 4: Review your wrong answers only.**
When doing practice questions, spend your review time on the questions you got wrong. Re-reading questions you answered correctly is one of the biggest time-wasters in student revision.

**Step 5: Protect your sleep and breaks.**
A study system that runs on sleep deprivation is self-defeating. Build your schedule so that studying is complete by a reasonable time. Breaks between sessions — even ten-minute breaks — improve consolidation and sustain attention.

## Time-Blocking for Students and Adults

Time-blocking means assigning specific tasks to specific time slots in your calendar, rather than working through a vague to-do list.

For students, a useful structure is:
- **Subject blocks of 45–60 minutes**, with a 10-minute break between.
- **Priority first:** tackle the most demanding or disliked subject when cognitive energy is highest — usually in the morning or early afternoon.
- **Active recall first:** begin each block with retrieval before any re-reading.
- **Clear end time:** knowing when study ends reduces procrastination at the start.

For adult learners returning to study, time-blocking is especially important because attention is more fragmented by competing responsibilities. Even two focused 45-minute blocks per day, used well, will outperform four hours of distracted studying.

## The Pomodoro Technique Adapted for Different Ages

The Pomodoro Technique — 25 minutes of focused work followed by a 5-minute break — is a useful scaffold, but the optimal interval varies by age and task type.

| Age / Stage | Focused work interval | Break | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary 1–2 | 10–15 minutes | 5–10 minutes | Attention spans are genuinely short; do not push |
| Primary 3–4 | 15–20 minutes | 5–10 minutes | Build up gradually |
| Primary 5–6 | 20–30 minutes | 5–10 minutes | Suitable for most exam prep tasks |
| Secondary 1–2 | 25–35 minutes | 5–10 minutes | Standard Pomodoro works well |
| Secondary 3–4 | 35–45 minutes | 10 minutes | Longer blocks for sustained problem-solving |
| Adult learner | 45–60 minutes | 10–15 minutes | Adjust for cognitive load of material |

The break is not optional. Brief mental rest — especially if it involves movement or something non-screen-based — restores attention and aids consolidation. A short walk is more restorative than scrolling.

## Quick-Reference Strategy Table

| Strategy | What it looks like in practice | Difficulty to implement | Time cost | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Retrieval practice | Flashcards, blank-page recall, practice questions without notes | Low–Medium | Low–Medium | Very High |
| Spaced practice | Revision calendar with revisits at increasing intervals | Medium | Medium | Very High |
| Interleaving | Mixed-topic problem sets; alternating subjects within a session | Medium | Medium | High |
| Elaborative interrogation | Asking "why?" during study; generating explanations | Low | Low | High |
| Concrete examples | Generating personal examples for each abstract concept | Low | Low | High |
| Dual coding | Sketching diagrams and concept maps alongside verbal notes | Low | Low–Medium | High |
| Re-reading | Reading notes again | Very Low | Medium | Low |
| Highlighting | Marking text with colour | Very Low | Low | Very Low |
| Cramming | Massed study immediately before an exam | Low | High | Very Low (for long-term) |

**How to read this table:** The most effective strategies are not the hardest ones — retrieval practice and spaced practice are straightforward to implement and have the highest returns. The main barrier is that they feel less comfortable than re-reading. That discomfort is, as noted throughout this knowledge base, precisely the point: productive difficulty is the learning.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The six high-utility strategies are: retrieval practice, spaced practice, interleaving, elaborative interrogation, concrete examples, and dual coding.
- Re-reading, highlighting, and cramming feel productive but deliver poor long-term retention.
- Build a personal study system around scheduled retrieval, not scheduled re-reading.
- Time-blocking with clear end times reduces procrastination.
- Adapt the Pomodoro technique to the learner's age — younger children need shorter focused intervals.
- The most effective strategies are also the most commonly avoided, because they require genuine effort.
