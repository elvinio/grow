# Using AI for Learning

## The Right Mental Model: Learning Partner, Not Oracle

Before anything else, let us establish the right way to think about AI tools in education. Two wrong models are extremely common:

**The cheat tool model**: "AI writes the essay / solves the problem / does my homework for me." This is the approach that produces short-term relief and long-term intellectual weakness. If you outsource the thinking, you do not get the thinking. You get a plausible-sounding output that you cannot evaluate, defend, or build on.

**The oracle model**: "AI knows the answer — I just need to ask it." This is subtly more dangerous, because AI outputs often sound authoritative while being factually wrong. Language models are trained to produce fluent, confident text. Confidence and accuracy are entirely different things.

The right model is **learning partner**. A good learning partner explains things in different ways until you understand, asks you questions to check your understanding, gives you honest feedback on your work, suggests what you should study next, and admits when they are not sure. Modern AI tools can do all of these things — when you use them correctly.

The key shift is this: AI should be making you think harder, not less. If your AI interaction ends with you having an answer, ask yourself: do you understand why that is the answer? Could you reproduce the reasoning? If not, you have received output, not learning.

## What AI Tools Are Available to Students

The AI education landscape has developed rapidly. Here are the tools most relevant to Singapore students in 2025:

**ChatGPT (OpenAI)** — The most widely used general-purpose AI. GPT-4o is excellent for explaining concepts, generating practice questions, and giving feedback on writing. Requires an account; the free tier is functional but has usage limits.

**Claude (Anthropic)** — Strong for nuanced explanations, working through complex reasoning, and giving detailed feedback on longer pieces of writing. Many educators find Claude's responses more careful and less prone to confident-sounding errors than some alternatives.

**Khan Academy Khanmigo** — Specifically designed for education. Rather than giving answers, Khanmigo asks questions that guide you toward understanding. This Socratic approach makes it harder to abuse as a shortcut and genuinely more effective for learning. Available within Khan Academy.

**Perplexity AI** — An AI search engine that gives answers with cited sources. Significantly reduces the hallucination problem because claims are linked to verifiable web sources. Very useful for research tasks where you need to check facts.

**Google NotebookLM** — Upload your own documents (notes, textbooks, past papers) and ask questions about them. Extremely useful for students who want to study their own material more actively — "quiz me on chapter 3" or "what are the key differences between these two concepts in my notes?"

## Effective Prompting Strategies

Most students use AI poorly because they treat it like a search engine — type a question, get an answer, move on. Effective use of AI for learning requires deliberate prompting. Here are strategies that actually work:

### Ask for Explanations, Not Answers

Instead of: "What is the water cycle?"

Try: "Explain the water cycle to me as if I have never heard of it before. Use a real-world example from Singapore's climate."

The second prompt gives you something you can engage with, ask follow-up questions about, and actually remember.

### Use "Teach Me Like I'm 12" Prompting

When a concept feels abstract or jargon-heavy, explicitly ask for a simpler explanation:

> "Explain opportunity cost to me like I'm 12, using an example from everyday life in Singapore."

Then, once you have the intuition, you can ask for the more formal definition. Building understanding from intuition to precision is far more effective than starting with textbook language.

### Ask AI to Quiz You

One of the most underused features of AI for studying:

> "I have an O-level Chemistry exam on acids and bases next week. Quiz me with five questions at the level I should expect on the exam. After each answer I give, tell me if I'm right and explain what I got wrong."

This turns passive reading into active retrieval — one of the most evidence-backed study techniques in cognitive science. You can do this for any subject, at any level.

### Chain of Thought: Ask AI to Show Its Reasoning

When working through maths or science problems:

> "Solve this problem step by step, explaining why you are taking each step: [problem]. After solving it, identify the concept I need to understand to solve similar problems on my own."

This is far more useful than just getting the answer, because it gives you a model of expert reasoning you can internalise.

### Ask AI to Challenge Your Thinking

> "I think the main cause of the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis was currency speculation. What am I missing? What would historians who disagree with this say?"

This type of prompting actively develops critical thinking and helps you see multiple perspectives — essential for humanities subjects and GP in particular.

### Get Analogies for Hard Concepts

> "I cannot get my head around recursion in programming. Give me three analogies from everyday life that might help me understand it."

Analogies are how the brain connects new information to existing knowledge. AI is genuinely good at generating multiple analogies and letting you find the one that clicks for you.

## Critical Evaluation: AI Hallucinations and How to Catch Them

This is non-negotiable: AI language models produce plausible-sounding text that is sometimes completely wrong. This is not a bug that will soon be fixed — it is an inherent property of how these systems work. They predict what text should come next; they do not look things up in a database of verified facts.

Common failure modes to watch for:

- **Fabricated citations**: AI will confidently cite papers, books, and articles that do not exist. Always verify any specific citation before using it.
- **Wrong numbers and statistics**: Population figures, historical dates, scientific constants — AI gets these wrong regularly. Check them.
- **Outdated information**: AI training data has a cutoff date. Anything that has changed recently may be wrong.
- **Overconfident analysis**: AI rarely says "I don't know" — it fills the gap with something plausible. Be especially cautious with niche or specialised topics.

The practical rule: use AI to understand and explore, but verify specific facts from authoritative sources (textbooks, MOE syllabi, established reference sites, peer-reviewed journals). For exam preparation especially, trust the past paper mark schemes and your teacher's guidance over AI explanations of specific syllabus points.

## Using AI to Improve Your Writing (Not to Write for You)

AI is an extraordinary writing coach — if you use it correctly. The correct use is to get feedback on your own writing, not to generate writing for you.

The workflow that works:

1. Write your own draft first, no AI involved.
2. Submit your draft to AI with a specific feedback request: "This is my GP essay. Give me feedback on: clarity of my thesis, whether my examples actually support my argument, and whether my conclusion adds something new or just repeats the introduction."
3. Read the feedback. Decide which suggestions you agree with and which you do not.
4. Revise the draft yourself, incorporating the feedback you found useful.
5. If you are unsure about a specific sentence or paragraph, ask: "Is this sentence clear? Does it say what I think it says?"

This approach develops your writing skills rapidly because you are still doing the thinking and the writing — you are just getting better feedback than most students have access to. It is analogous to having a patient, available writing tutor.

What you should not do: ask AI to "improve" your essay and then submit the result. Beyond the academic integrity issues, this approach teaches you nothing and produces writing that does not sound like you — something experienced teachers often notice.

## Academic Integrity: AI Use in Singapore Schools

MOE's stance on AI in schools is actively evolving. As of 2025, the general framework is:

- AI is not prohibited outright, but using AI to complete assessed work and submitting it as your own is considered academic dishonesty — equivalent to plagiarism.
- The spirit of assessments is that they measure your learning. If AI did the learning and you just submitted the output, the grade does not reflect your ability.
- Some teachers and schools are beginning to design assessments that explicitly incorporate AI use as part of the task — for example, asking students to use AI to generate a draft and then critically evaluate and revise it.

When in doubt about a specific assignment, ask your teacher directly: "Is it acceptable to use AI tools for this task, and if so, in what way?" This is not a suspicious question — it shows maturity and integrity.

The broader principle: use AI to help you understand and improve, never to replace your own thinking in work that is meant to demonstrate your thinking.

## AI for Adult Learners

Parents and educators can benefit significantly from AI tools in their own learning and work:

- **Summarising long documents**: Paste a lengthy government report, research paper, or policy document and ask AI to give you a structured summary of the key points and recommendations.
- **Explaining unfamiliar terms**: Encountered jargon in your child's textbook that you do not recognise? Ask AI to explain it in plain language.
- **Creating personalised study plans**: "My child has A-level Chemistry in 8 weeks and is weakest on organic chemistry mechanisms. Can you suggest a realistic revision plan?"
- **Understanding new technologies**: Ask AI to explain how something works — blockchain, machine learning, quantum computing — in terms accessible to a non-expert.
- **Preparing for conversations**: Before a parent-teacher meeting, ask AI what questions you should consider asking to get a useful picture of your child's progress.

## How to Talk to Your Child About AI

The most important conversation you can have with a young person about AI is not about rules — it is about mindset.

**Encourage curiosity, not dependency.** "What did you use AI for today? Did it surprise you with something? Did it get anything wrong?" These questions signal that AI is a tool worth engaging with critically, not just a black box that produces answers.

**Talk about verification.** Make it normal to check AI-generated information: "That's interesting — how would we check if that's actually true?"

**Discuss the effort question.** "If the AI did the thinking, what did you learn?" This is not an accusatory question — it is a genuine one that helps young people develop their own internal compass about appropriate use.

**Share your own AI use.** If you use AI tools at work or for personal tasks, talk about it. What do you use it for? When do you find it helpful? When do you not trust it? Modelling critical, thoughtful AI use is more powerful than any lecture.

## The Long Game: AI Literacy as a 21st-Century Skill

The students who will thrive in the coming decades are not those who resist AI or those who delegate everything to it. They are those who know how to work with AI effectively — directing it, evaluating its output, knowing its limits, and combining its capabilities with distinctly human skills: creativity, ethical judgment, deep domain expertise, and the ability to communicate with other humans.

AI literacy is not a single skill. It is a cluster of habits:
- Knowing how to formulate good prompts (clear, specific, contextual)
- Evaluating AI output critically rather than accepting it at face value
- Understanding at a conceptual level how AI systems work and what they are optimised for
- Knowing when AI is and is not the right tool for a task
- Maintaining the intellectual habits — deep reading, sustained attention, original reasoning — that AI cannot replicate

These habits are best developed early and reinforced consistently.

## Singapore Context: A Nation Investing in AI Literacy

Singapore is exceptionally well-positioned in the AI era. The Smart Nation initiative has made digital and AI literacy a national priority. Key resources and programmes:

- **AI Singapore (AISG)** — [aisingapore.org](https://aisingapore.org) — Runs AI apprenticeship programmes, the AI for Everyone (AI4E) course (free, online, accessible to all ages), and supports AI research and industry development.
- **IMDA Digital for Life** — Ongoing national movement supporting digital inclusion and literacy, with programmes for students, seniors, and working adults.
- **SkillsFuture AI courses** — For adult learners, a wide range of AI and data literacy courses with SkillsFuture Credit funding available.
- **MOE's EdTech initiatives** — Schools are increasingly incorporating AI tools and concepts into the curriculum. Keep an eye on MOE's Student Learning Space for updated resources.

Singapore students have access to world-class resources, a government actively investing in AI education, and an economy that will reward AI-literate professionals. The opportunity is significant — and it starts with building the right habits now.
