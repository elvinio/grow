# English

English occupies a unique position in Singapore. It is the language of instruction, the language of the workplace, and the first language of many homes — yet many students still struggle with it in the ways that examinations measure. This is partly because strong conversational English and strong academic English are different skills. This guide addresses both, and covers practical strategies for every component of the PSLE and O-level English examinations.

---

## The Four Skills: Reading, Writing, Listening, Speaking

English proficiency is built on four interlocking skills. Weakness in any one area creates a ceiling on the others.

- **Reading** is the most powerful single driver of English development. Students who read widely have larger vocabularies, better grammar intuition, and more sophisticated ideas to draw on when writing. Reading cannot be replaced by any other activity.
- **Writing** is where reading comes to life. It requires ideas, structure, sentence control, and vocabulary — all of which are fed by reading. Writing is also the skill most directly tested in high-stakes exams.
- **Listening** is undertaught but critical. Active listening — attending to how a speaker structures an argument, uses transitions, or modulates tone — develops both comprehension and speaking skills.
- **Speaking** in Singapore carries the added complexity of code-switching between Singlish and Standard English. Oral components in exams test formal register, which many students are not regularly exposed to at home.

A well-rounded approach to English works on all four, not just exam-relevant writing.

## Reading Widely: The Foundation

No single habit develops English ability more effectively than wide reading. But not all reading is equal.

**What to read:**
- For primary students: picture books, chapter books, and age-appropriate fiction that stretches vocabulary slightly. Series like Roald Dahl, Diary of a Wimpy Kid, or Percy Jackson build reading stamina and make reading feel rewarding.
- For secondary students: a mix of literary fiction, quality non-fiction (popular science, biography, history), and regular news reading. The Straits Times is excellent for vocabulary exposure and current affairs.
- Across all ages: poetry, which develops sensitivity to language at the level of the word and the sentence.

**How to read for development, not just pleasure:**
- Read without a dictionary first — try to infer meaning from context.
- Revisit a passage and look up words that could not be inferred.
- Keep a reading journal: note interesting words, striking phrases, or ideas that surprised you.
- Discuss books with someone. Articulating your response to a text deepens comprehension.

The National Library Board's ReadSG programme and the extensive NLB membership network make borrowing books accessible and free for all Singaporeans.

## Comprehension Strategies: Annotation, Inference, Close Reading

Comprehension is not just reading and answering questions. It is an active process that can be explicitly taught and practised.

**Annotation:** Train students to mark up the text as they read. Underline key ideas. Circle unfamiliar words. Put a question mark where something is unclear. Write a brief note in the margin summarising each paragraph. This slows down reading enough to actually engage with the text, rather than skimming for the answer.

**Inference:** Many comprehension questions ask what the text *implies*, not what it *says*. Inference requires reading what is present and reasoning about what is absent. Practise by asking: "What does this tell us that the author has not directly stated? What must be true for this sentence to make sense?"

**Close reading:** For literary passages, pay attention to word choice, tone, and structure. Why did the author choose "stumbled" rather than "walked"? What does the length of a sentence tell you about pace or tension? O-level Literature and Language papers both reward this kind of attention.

**For parents:** After your child reads a passage, ask open-ended questions: "What was the writer trying to make you feel? Did it work for you? What surprised you?" This develops the reflective reading that inference tasks require.

## Composition Techniques: Planning, PEEL, Varied Structures

Many students sit down to write a composition and start with the first word that comes to mind. This produces unfocused, repetitive writing. Good writing begins before writing.

**Planning (5–10 minutes for a 45-minute composition):**
- Choose the angle or perspective first.
- Sketch a brief outline: introduction, 2–3 body points or scenes, conclusion.
- Decide the tone and register.
- Note 3–5 strong vocabulary words you want to use.

**The PEEL structure** (Point, Evidence/Elaboration, Explanation, Link) is a reliable scaffold for argumentative writing:
- **Point:** State your argument clearly.
- **Evidence/Elaboration:** Support it with an example, fact, or developed scenario.
- **Explanation:** Explain *why* this supports your point.
- **Link:** Connect back to the question or forward to the next paragraph.

PEEL is a scaffold, not a straitjacket. Once students can write structured paragraphs consistently, encourage them to vary the order for effect.

**Sentence variety:** A composition written entirely in simple sentences reads as flat and immature. Mix short punchy sentences with longer, subordinate-clause constructions. Use varied sentence openings: adverbial phrases ("Just as the rain began to fall..."), participial phrases ("Exhausted from the climb..."), direct speech.

**Show, don't tell:** Instead of "She was very sad," write "She stared at the empty chair, running her thumb over the frayed edge of his photograph." Examiner rubrics reward vivid, specific writing.

## Oral Communication: Formal vs Informal Registers

Singapore is a multilingual, multi-register society. Most students effortlessly switch between registers informally — with friends at recess, at home with family — but have limited practice with the formal register that oral examinations require.

**Formal register in PSLE and O-level oral:**
- Full sentences, not fragments.
- Standard pronunciation — not wrong pronunciation, but the non-colloquial variety.
- Structured responses: introduce your point, elaborate, conclude.
- Active listening when given a stimulus (a photo, a passage) — notice details and be specific.

**Building formal spoken English:**
- Read aloud for 10 minutes daily. This builds fluency, models sentence structures, and develops awareness of how formal written language sounds.
- Have dinner table discussions on a topic (a news story, an ethical question, a personal experience) in full sentences.
- Participate in debates, presentations, or drama activities at school.

**Code-switching** — moving fluently between Singlish and Standard English — is actually a sophisticated skill. The goal is not to eliminate Singlish (which plays an important cultural and social function) but to develop the ability to choose the appropriate register for the context.

## Singapore English (Singlish) vs Standard English

Singlish is not "bad English." It is a contact language with its own grammar, phonology, and vocabulary — and it is an important part of Singapore's cultural identity. But it is also not Standard English, and conflating the two creates exam problems.

Common Singlish features that need to be managed in formal writing:
- Particles: "lah," "leh," "lor," "meh" — these do not belong in formal written or spoken English.
- Topic-prominent structure: "This problem, many students also have" should be "Many students also have this problem."
- Missing articles: "She went to hospital" should be "She went to the hospital."
- Reduplication: "Can can" or "fast fast" — informal intensification that does not appear in Standard English.

Teaching students to recognise these patterns — without stigmatising Singlish — allows them to make deliberate choices about register.

## PSLE English: Components and Strategies

PSLE English is assessed across four papers:
- **Paper 1 (Writing):** Situational writing (email, letter, report) and continuous writing (narrative or expository composition). Focus: task fulfilment, audience awareness, organisation, language accuracy.
- **Paper 2 (Language Use and Comprehension):** Grammar MCQ, vocabulary cloze, grammar cloze, editing for spelling and grammar, comprehension, and visual text comprehension.
- **Paper 3 (Listening Comprehension):** Audio passages with questions. Practise actively listening — not just hearing.
- **Paper 4 (Oral Communication):** Reading aloud and stimulus-based conversation.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Situational writing: not addressing the purpose, audience, or format correctly.
- Comprehension: copying whole sentences from the passage instead of paraphrasing and condensing.
- Composition: starting in the middle of the action (good) but losing focus by the end.

## O-Level English: Situational Writing, Essays, Comprehension

O-level English (1128) covers:
- **Paper 1:** Situational writing (formal and informal) and argumentative/expository essays. At this level, the quality of argumentation matters — claims need to be supported, counterarguments considered.
- **Paper 2:** Reading comprehension (summary writing, application question, inference), and language analysis (how writers use language for effect).

**Summary writing** is a high-value skill that many students underperform in. The discipline is to capture only the relevant information in your own words, within the word limit, without including your own opinion or examples not in the passage.

**The application question** asks students to apply a concept from the passage to their own context. Practise having a bank of real-world examples from current affairs, personal experience, and reading.

## Building Vocabulary Organically

Vocabulary lists have limited value on their own. Words learned in context — through reading, listening, and discussion — are far more durable and usable.

**Effective vocabulary development:**
- When you encounter an unfamiliar word, note the sentence it appears in. This gives you a sense of register, collocations, and grammatical behaviour.
- Aim to encounter a new word multiple times before considering it learned. Reading widely makes this happen naturally.
- For exam vocabulary, focus on high-frequency academic words (the Academic Word List is a useful reference) rather than obscure or flashy words.
- Use new words in writing and speech within a day or two of learning them.
