# Fermentation: Kimchi, Pickles, Yogurt and Simple Ferments

Fermentation is one of the oldest food preservation techniques in the world, and it has a reputation for being either intimidating or the exclusive territory of people with abundant counter space and a very particular lifestyle. Neither is true. The basics are simple, the equipment requirements are minimal, and the results are better than most things you can buy.

---

## What Fermentation Actually Is

Fermentation is the process of microorganisms — bacteria, yeast, or both — transforming food. They consume sugars and produce acids, alcohol, or CO₂ as by-products. The results: food that keeps longer, tastes more complex, and is often more nutritious than the original.

There are several types:
- **Lacto-fermentation**: beneficial bacteria (lactobacillus) produce lactic acid, which preserves the food and gives it a sour flavour. Used for kimchi, sauerkraut, pickles, and fermented vegetables generally.
- **Acetic acid fermentation**: yeast converts sugars to alcohol, then bacteria convert alcohol to acetic acid — vinegar.
- **Yeast fermentation**: yeast consumes sugars and produces CO₂ and alcohol. Used in bread (sourdough), beer, wine, kombucha.
- **Lactic acid + time**: yogurt and certain cheeses are made with bacteria that transform milk lactose into lactic acid, thickening and souring the milk.

You don't need to memorise the chemistry. What matters is: salt and beneficial bacteria keep food safe; acidity is a preservative; and in most of these processes, time does the work.

---

## Why Bother at Home?

Three reasons:

1. **Cost**: a jar of decent kimchi at the supermarket costs several dollars. A kilogram of napa cabbage from the wet market costs almost nothing, and the other ingredients are cheap staples. The economics of homemade fermented foods are excellent.
2. **Quality and control**: you control the ingredients, salt level, and fermentation time. Commercial kimchi is often too sweet or too mild. Homemade yogurt is fresher and tangier than most store-bought options.
3. **It's genuinely not difficult**: most ferments require about 20–30 minutes of active work, then patience. You're not doing much — you're creating conditions for microorganisms to do their work and then getting out of the way.

---

## The Safety Basics

Lacto-fermentation is one of the safest forms of food preservation. The mechanism is self-protecting: salt suppresses harmful bacteria while allowing salt-tolerant lactobacillus to thrive. As the lactobacillus produces lactic acid, the environment becomes increasingly acidic, which further prevents spoilage organisms from surviving.

The key rules:
- **Keep everything submerged in brine**: vegetables exposed to air can grow mould. Keep them under the liquid at all times.
- **Use the right salt concentration**: too little salt and harmful bacteria can outcompete the beneficial ones; too much and fermentation stalls. For most vegetable ferments, 2–3% salt by weight of water is the range.
- **Trust your nose**: lacto-fermented vegetables smell sour — pleasantly so, like a mild vinegar. If something smells putrid, sulphurous beyond a brief initial period, or in any way off rather than just sour, discard it. Mould on the surface (but not on submerged vegetables) can often be skimmed off if the brine below is clear and sour-smelling. When in doubt, throw it out.
- **Non-iodised salt**: iodine in table salt inhibits bacterial activity. Use sea salt, kosher salt, or pickling salt.

## Quick Pickles (Ready in 30 Minutes to 24 Hours)

Quick pickles are not fermented in the true sense — they use vinegar rather than producing their own acid. But they're fast, reliable, and immediately useful in the kitchen. They add brightness and crunch to heavier meals and keep for 2–4 weeks in the fridge.

**The basic brine ratio**:
- 1 cup water
- 1 cup rice vinegar (or white vinegar for a sharper result)
- 1 tsp salt
- 1–2 tsp sugar

Bring to a boil to dissolve the salt and sugar, pour over your sliced vegetables in a clean jar, cool to room temperature, and refrigerate.

**What to pickle**:
- Cucumber: thinly sliced or cut into spears; ready in an hour
- Carrot and daikon: julienned or coin-sliced; ready in a few hours, better overnight
- Red onion: thinly sliced; ready in 30 minutes, turns a beautiful pink
- Chilli: sliced rings; ready in 2–3 hours
- Cabbage: thinly shredded; ready overnight

Add flavour to the brine: a few peppercorns, a slice of ginger, a clove of garlic, a small chilli. These are background notes, not dominant flavours, but they make a difference.

Use quick pickles alongside braised meats, fried rice, noodle soups — anywhere you want a sharp, acidic contrast to something rich.

## Lacto-Fermented Vegetables (True Fermentation, 3–7 Days)

This is the real thing — vegetables fermented in salt brine, no vinegar added.

**The brine**: 20–30g non-iodised salt per litre of water (2–3% by weight). Use clean, filtered or boiled-and-cooled water if your tap water is heavily chlorinated, as chlorine inhibits fermentation. Singapore tap water is treated and should be allowed to sit for 20–30 minutes, or briefly boiled, if you want to be careful.

**The method**:
1. Prepare your vegetables: whole chillies, garlic cloves, cucumber spears, cauliflower florets, radish, green papaya, or a mix
2. Pack tightly into a clean glass jar (a reused jam jar works perfectly)
3. Pour brine over the vegetables until fully submerged
4. Weigh the vegetables down to keep them under the brine — a zip-lock bag filled with additional brine laid flat over the vegetables works well
5. Cover loosely (a cloth secured with a rubber band, or a jar lid set on top but not sealed) to allow CO₂ to escape without letting contaminants in
6. Leave at room temperature (in Singapore, this means somewhere not too hot — away from direct sunlight and not next to your oven)
7. Taste daily after day 3; move to the fridge when it's sour to your liking

Singapore's ambient temperature (typically 26–30°C indoors) accelerates fermentation compared to cooler climates. What might take 5–7 days in a European kitchen might be ready in 3–4 days here.

The finished product keeps for months in the fridge, continuing to slowly develop in flavour.

## Kimchi (Beginner Version)

Kimchi is fermented napa cabbage (wombok) with Korean chilli flakes, garlic, ginger, and fish sauce. It's one of the most flavourful things you can make at home and one of the most forgiving.

**What you need**:
- 1 medium napa cabbage (about 1–1.5kg)
- 3–4 tbsp non-iodised salt (for the initial salting)
- 3–4 tbsp gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes)
- 4–5 cloves garlic, minced or grated
- 1 tbsp fresh ginger, grated
- 2 tbsp fish sauce (or soy sauce for a vegan version)
- 3–4 spring onions, cut into 3cm pieces
- Optional: 1 cup daikon, julienned

**Where to find gochugaru in Singapore**: Korean supermarkets including K-Village (Tanjong Pagar), Shine Korea (various outlets), or online via Lazada or Shopee. Some Cold Storage branches also stock it.

**The method**:
1. Quarter the cabbage lengthwise, then cut crosswise into 5cm pieces
2. Toss with 3 tbsp salt in a large bowl; massage briefly and let sit for 1–2 hours, turning occasionally. The cabbage will wilt and release liquid — this draws out moisture and seasons the vegetable.
3. Rinse the cabbage thoroughly in cold water (taste a piece — it should be pleasantly salty, not overwhelmingly so), then drain and squeeze out excess moisture
4. In the same bowl, combine gochugaru, garlic, ginger, and fish sauce. Mix to form a rough paste.
5. Add the cabbage, spring onion, and daikon if using. Mix thoroughly — gloves are recommended; gochugaru stains.
6. Pack tightly into clean glass jars, pressing down so the cabbage compacts and releases liquid. Leave 3–4cm of headspace for CO₂ expansion.
7. Close the lids and leave at room temperature for 1–2 days. Press the kimchi down once or twice daily to keep it submerged in its juices.
8. Transfer to the fridge once it smells tangy and tastes pleasantly sour. It will continue to improve for several weeks.

Fresh (just made) kimchi is crunchy and mild. After 2–3 weeks in the fridge it's fully fermented and more complex. After 6–8 weeks it's aged kimchi — excellent for cooking (kimchi fried rice, kimchi jjigae) where deep, pungent flavour is an asset.

## Yogurt (Heat + Culture)

Yogurt is made by introducing specific bacteria to warm milk and keeping it at a stable temperature long enough for fermentation to complete. The process takes about 8 hours of mostly hands-off time.

**The method**:
1. Pour 1 litre of full-fat milk into a saucepan. Heat to 82°C — this kills competing bacteria and changes the protein structure for a thicker result. A thermometer helps here.
2. Cool the milk to 43°C (it should feel warm but not uncomfortably hot on your wrist). This is the optimal temperature for lactobacillus without killing it.
3. Stir in 2 tbsp of live plain yogurt as your starter. The live cultures in the yogurt will multiply and ferment the milk.
4. Pour into a clean glass jar or container. Wrap in a thick towel or place in a slightly warm oven — oven light on, door closed, is enough warmth in Singapore's climate.
5. Leave undisturbed for 6–8 hours. Check for a firm, custard-like set. If it's still liquid, give it 1–2 more hours.
6. Refrigerate once set. It will firm up further as it cools.

For Greek-style yogurt: line a sieve with muslin or a clean thin cloth, pour the yogurt in, and let it drain in the fridge for 2–4 hours. What drains out is whey (save it for smoothies or bread dough). What's left is thick, tangy strained yogurt.

**Sustaining your starter**: use a few spoonfuls of the last batch as the starter for the next. You can keep this going indefinitely. Commercial yogurt starters weaken over time in home kitchens — if your yogurt becomes runny after a few batches, buy a fresh commercial yogurt to refresh the culture.

In Singapore's heat, yogurt set on the counter (not in the oven) may ferment faster — check at 5–6 hours rather than 8.

## Sourdough Starter (The Long Game)

A sourdough starter is a live culture of wild yeast and bacteria cultivated from flour. Once active, it can leaven bread without commercial yeast and adds complex flavour that instant yeast cannot replicate.

**Starting from scratch**:
1. Mix 50g plain flour (or a mix of plain and whole wheat — the bran carries more wild yeast) and 50g water in a clean jar. Stir well and cover loosely.
2. For the next 5–7 days, once daily: discard half the mixture, then add 50g fresh flour and 50g water. Mix well, cover, and leave.
3. After a few days you'll see small bubbles. After 5–7 days the starter should double in size within 4–8 hours of feeding and smell pleasantly sour and yeasty. It's ready to use.

**Singapore challenge**: Singapore's heat and humidity mean fermentation is fast. Your starter may be active in 3–5 days rather than 7. Check it more frequently and feed it if it starts to smell very acidic or you see a grey liquid layer on top (called "hooch" — just stir it back in or pour it off; it means the starter is hungry).

**Maintaining it**: once active, a starter kept at room temperature needs daily feeding. If you bake weekly, keep it in the fridge and feed it once a week before baking. Bring it out, feed it, let it peak (the point where it has risen and is just starting to fall), then use.

**Beyond bread**: an active starter can make fluffy pancakes, crackers, pizza dough, and waffles. These are great uses for the "discard" you'd otherwise throw away during the daily feeding phase.

## Fermented Condiments Worth Trying

Once you're comfortable with the basics, these are small projects with outsized rewards:

- **Fermented chilli sauce**: blend fresh chillies (bird's eye, red Thai, or a mix), pack into a jar with 2–3% brine, and ferment for 3–5 days at room temperature. The result is a complex, tangy hot sauce far more interesting than commercial versions.
- **Fermented garlic in honey**: peel an entire head of garlic, pack into a jar, cover with raw honey. The garlic releases moisture and ferments slowly over 4–6 weeks, becoming mild, sweet, and deeply flavoured. The honey becomes a complex garlic syrup.
- **Miso**: a longer project (weeks to months), but deeply satisfying. Made from cooked soybeans, salt, and koji (a mould-inoculated grain). Koji is available at Japanese supermarkets and online. The result is genuine homemade miso — far more complex than anything you can buy at most Singapore supermarkets.

## Equipment You Actually Need

- **Glass jars**: reuse jam jars. Wide-mouth mason jars (sold at Daiso and online) are more convenient but not essential.
- **Kitchen scale**: necessary for getting brine percentages right, especially for lacto-fermentation. Not expensive — a basic digital scale costs under $15.
- **Instant-read thermometer**: essential for yogurt and useful for checking meat and bread temperatures too. Worth having in any home kitchen.
- **Muslin or cheesecloth**: for straining yogurt. Can substitute with a very clean thin cotton cloth.

That is genuinely the complete list. Fermentation needs time and salt, not specialist equipment. Start with quick pickles or yogurt — both are ready within a day and both will work the first time. Build from there.
