# Cooking Fundamentals: Heat, Salt, Fat & Acid

Good cooking is not about following recipes perfectly. It is about understanding a small set of principles that explain why food tastes the way it does — and how to fix it when it does not. Those principles are heat, salt, fat, and acid. Master these four, and you can cook anything.

This is not theory for its own sake. Every decision you make at the stove — when to add the garlic, whether to deglaze the pan, why the dish tastes flat — traces back to one of these four elements. Learn them and you stop guessing.

---

## Heat: Transformation, Not Just Cooking

Heat is what changes raw ingredients into food. But the type of heat, and how you apply it, determines whether something tastes extraordinary or merely edible.

### The Maillard Reaction

When protein and sugars are exposed to high, dry heat (roughly 140–165°C), they undergo a cascade of chemical reactions that produce hundreds of flavour compounds — the brown crust on seared chicken thighs, the colour on a properly cooked steak, the toasty edges of roasted onions. This is the Maillard reaction, and it is the single biggest source of deep, savoury flavour in cooking.

You cannot get the Maillard reaction from a wet, steaming pan. The surface of the food must be dry and the heat must be high. If there is moisture in the pan — from oil that is too cold, from food that is wet, or from overcrowding — the food steams instead and turns grey-beige. Edible, but flat.

### Caramelisation

Separate from Maillard, caramelisation is what happens when sugars are heated on their own. The slow-cooked onions that turn jammy and dark after 25 minutes, the roasted carrots with crispy sweet edges — that is caramelisation. It starts around 160°C for fructose and higher for sucrose. It takes patience and dry heat.

### Protein Denaturation

Proteins tighten and contract when heated. Cook a chicken breast past 75°C and the proteins seize up, squeezing out moisture — that is why overcooked chicken is dry and rubbery. Cook a soft-boiled egg at 63°C and the white sets while the yolk stays custardy. Understanding that temperature, not time alone, determines doneness is one of the most useful things you can know.

### Dry Heat vs Moist Heat

- **Dry heat** (searing, roasting, broiling, stir-frying): uses high temperatures, produces browning, drives off surface moisture. Best for things you want colour and crust on.
- **Moist heat** (simmering, braising, steaming, pressure cooking): uses water or steam, gentler, prevents the surface from drying out. Best for tough cuts that need time to break down collagen, or for delicate proteins like fish and tofu.

Neither is better — they serve different purposes. Many dishes use both in sequence: sear a chicken thigh first (dry heat for colour), then braise it in liquid (moist heat for tenderness).

## Salt: The Most Powerful Ingredient in Your Kitchen

Salt does not just make food salty. At the right level, it suppresses bitterness, enhances sweetness, amplifies every other flavour, and changes the texture of proteins and vegetables. Using too little salt is the most common reason home-cooked food tastes flat.

### What Salt Does

- **Suppresses bitterness**: a pinch of salt in coffee or dark chocolate reduces perceived bitterness dramatically. Same with bitter greens.
- **Draws out moisture**: salt on cut vegetables (cucumbers, cabbage) draws out water via osmosis. Useful when you want crunch (salting and draining cucumber before a salad) and when you do not (avoid salting mushrooms early — they will steam in their own liquid instead of browning).
- **Seasons from within**: when you salt meat before cooking, the salt draws moisture out and then reabsorbs back in, carrying flavour into the flesh. This is why a chicken marinated with salt overnight tastes better than one seasoned at the table.

### When to Salt

- **Before cooking** (dry brine): salt meat at least 30 minutes before, ideally overnight in the fridge. The surface initially becomes damp, then the moisture reabsorbs. The result is seasoned, juicy meat.
- **During cooking**: season vegetables when they hit the pan; add salt to pasta water so generously it tastes like dilute seawater; season in layers as you build a dish.
- **After cooking** (finishing): a pinch of flaky sea salt on a finished dish adds texture, brightness, and a pop of flavour that coarser salt earlier in the process could not deliver.

### Tasting and Adjusting

Add a small amount, taste, wait 30 seconds, taste again. Salt perception takes a moment to register. If a dish tastes flat, add a small pinch of salt and a squeeze of acid before reaching for more chilli or sauce — most flat dishes are under-salted, not under-spiced.

### Common Salts

- **Table salt**: fine grind, dissolves quickly, iodised. Good for general cooking.
- **Kosher salt**: coarser, no iodine, easier to pinch and control. The professional kitchen standard.
- **Flaky sea salt** (Maldon, fleur de sel): for finishing only. Sprinkle over eggs, grilled fish, salads, or chocolate desserts just before eating.

## Fat: Flavour, Texture and Heat

Fat carries fat-soluble flavour compounds — many of the most aromatic components of garlic, spices, and herbs only release their flavour when in contact with fat. Without fat, food tastes thin.

### What Fat Does

- **Carries and transfers flavour**: blooming spices in oil releases their aromatics. Frying garlic in oil infuses the fat, which then flavours everything cooked in it.
- **Creates texture**: fat coats the palate, giving richness and body to sauces. Emulsified fats (like in a vinaigrette or a butter pan sauce) create silky, cohesive textures.
- **Conducts heat**: fat heats to temperatures well above water's boiling point, enabling browning. Water-based cooking is capped at 100°C; oil can reach 200°C+.

### Smoke Points Matter

When oil reaches its smoke point, it breaks down, produces acrid flavours, and releases harmful compounds. Match the fat to the task:

- **High-heat cooking** (searing, stir-frying, deep-frying): use neutral oils with high smoke points — sunflower oil, vegetable oil, rice bran oil. These have smoke points above 220°C.
- **Medium heat** (sautéing, gentle frying): olive oil is fine here. Its smoke point (190–210°C) is adequate for most stovetop cooking, though extra virgin olive oil is better saved for lower heat.
- **Flavour and basting**: butter is extraordinary for flavour — browned (beurre noisette) butter has a nutty, caramel depth. Baste a steak or fish fillet with foaming butter for the last minute of cooking.
- **Finishing only**: sesame oil, chilli oil, and other strongly flavoured oils should almost never be used for cooking. They burn easily and lose their character. Add them at the very end, off the heat.

## Acid: The Secret Ingredient Most Home Cooks Underuse

Acid brightens food. It makes flavours pop, cuts through richness, and lifts dishes that have gone dull. A squeeze of lime over a bowl of laksa, a splash of rice vinegar in a stir-fry, a dash of fish sauce in a curry — these small additions change everything.

### What Acid Does

- **Brightens and lifts**: acid activates your salivary glands and heightens perception of other flavours.
- **Balances richness**: fatty, heavy dishes (braised pork belly, a rich coconut curry) need acid to keep them from feeling cloying.
- **Finishes flat dishes**: if a dish tastes complete but somehow dull, add acid before adding more salt or spice. Often it is acid that is missing.

### Sources of Acid in the Singapore Kitchen

- **Citrus**: lime juice is the everyday workhorse. Lemon is less common in Southeast Asian cooking but useful for Western dishes.
- **Vinegar**: rice vinegar is mild and clean, good for dressings and marinades. Black vinegar (Zhenjiang/Chinkiang) is deeper and used in braises, dipping sauces, and fried rice.
- **Tamarind**: rich, fruity sour flavour — essential in asam dishes, Penang-style cooking, and tamarind-braised pork ribs.
- **Tomato**: both acidic and sweet. Cooked-down tomatoes add body and balance to curries and sauces.
- **Fish sauce**: technically a fermented condiment, but it brings both salt and acid to a dish. A few dashes can do more than adding either salt or acid separately.

### When to Add Acid

Add most acids near the end of cooking. Volatile aromatic acids (citrus juice especially) lose their brightness when exposed to prolonged heat. If you add lime juice at the beginning of a 30-minute braise, it will taste flat and washed-out by the end. Add it after the heat is off, or just before serving.

### The Salt + Acid Combo

The most reliable finishing move in cooking: taste a dish, add a small pinch of salt, squeeze of citrus or dash of vinegar, stir, taste again. This combination restores depth and brightness simultaneously and works on almost any savoury dish — soups, stews, stir-fries, grain bowls, salads.

## Mise en Place: Set Up Before You Cook

"Mise en place" is French for "everything in its place." It means having all your ingredients prepped, measured, and ready before you turn on the heat.

This is not a chef's pretension. It is the difference between calm, controlled cooking and frantic scrambling. On an induction stove especially, heat changes are fast. If you are still mincing garlic when the oil starts smoking, you will burn the garlic. If you have not measured your sauce ingredients when the protein is almost done, you will over-reduce it trying to rush.

Practical mise en place at home:
- Read the full recipe before you start
- Chop all aromatics (garlic, ginger, shallots, chilli) and put them in small bowls
- Mix any sauce ingredients or marinades in advance
- Have your salt, oil, and acid within arm's reach
- Plate your protein near the stove so you are not walking across the kitchen mid-sear

This habit takes five extra minutes before you cook. It saves you ten minutes of stress during.

## Tasting as You Go

Your palate is your most important piece of kitchen equipment. No recipe, however well written, can fully account for differences in your soy sauce brand, the saltiness of your stock, the ripeness of your tomatoes, or your personal threshold for spice. You must taste.

Taste at every stage:
- Before adding aromatics: does the oil smell right?
- After building your sauce base: enough salt? Enough acid?
- Mid-cook: is the liquid reducing too fast? Is the seasoning developing?
- Just before plating: what does it need? Usually one of — salt, acid, heat, or more time.

Develop the reflex of asking "what does this dish need?" rather than "what do I add next?" The answer is almost always one of the four fundamentals.

## The Singapore Home Pantry: What to Always Have Stocked

With these on hand, you can cook almost anything in a Singapore kitchen on short notice:

**Sauces and condiments**
- Light soy sauce — general seasoning, marinades, dipping sauce
- Dark soy sauce — colour, depth, slightly sweet; used in braises and fried rice
- Oyster sauce — rich, savoury, a little sweet; stir-fry finisher
- Fish sauce — salty, complex, acidic; used in Southeast Asian cooking
- Sesame oil — finishing only; adds nuttiness to noodles, soups, stir-fries
- Shaoxing rice wine — depth and complexity in marinades and braises

**Aromatics and spices**
- Garlic — fresh, always
- Ginger — fresh; peel a knob and keep it in the freezer, grate from frozen
- Dried chillies — for oil infusion and long-cooked dishes
- White pepper — sharper than black, used in most Chinese-style cooking
- Five spice — for braises and roasted pork

**Pantry staples**
- Cornstarch — thickens sauces, velvets meat for stir-fries
- Sugar (white and brown or palm sugar) — balances salt and acid
- Cooking oil (sunflower or vegetable) — neutral high-heat fat
- Rice (jasmine and/or short-grain) — always

**Acids**
- Limes — always fresh
- Rice vinegar
- Black vinegar (Chinkiang)

These ingredients cost very little to maintain and give you the building blocks for hundreds of dishes. Run out of one and you will notice immediately.
