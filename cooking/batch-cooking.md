# Batch Cooking: One Session, Five Days of Good Meals

Batch cooking is not about making food in advance and eating the same thing all week. It's about cooking components — a protein, a grain, a vegetable, a sauce — so that you have building blocks available when you're tired, hungry, and in no mood to start from scratch. Done well, it reduces decision fatigue, saves real money compared to daily takeaway, and means you eat better on the days when cooking feels like the last thing you want to do.

---

## The Mindset Shift: Components, Not Complete Dishes

The key change in how you think about batch cooking: stop trying to pre-cook five separate meals. Instead, cook flexible elements that can combine in different ways across the week.

A whole roast chicken, for example, is not just Sunday's dinner. It's:
- Day 1: sliced chicken over rice with broth and cucumber (chicken rice)
- Day 2: cold day-old rice + leftover chicken + egg = fried rice in 10 minutes
- Day 3: shredded chicken + broth + noodles = noodle soup
- Day 4: the carcass, simmered for an hour with ginger and spring onion, makes a full pot of stock

That's four meals from one piece of cooking work. The same logic applies to braised meats, cooked grains, roasted vegetables, and a pot of broth. Cook once, reconfigure throughout the week.

This is fundamentally different from cooking individual meal-sized portions and storing them. Components give you flexibility. Pre-assembled complete dishes require you to want that specific dish on that specific day — which is rarely how appetite works.

## The Weekly Batch Session

**When**: Sunday afternoon is the most natural fit for many people — the week is ahead, you're not yet tired, and there's time. But any 2–3 hour window works. Some people prefer a weeknight after dinner when the kitchen is already dirty anyway.

**The core principle of efficiency**: run all your equipment simultaneously, sequenced by cooking time. Start the longest-cooking items first, then prepare shorter-cooking items while those run.

A well-organised session might look like this:

- **Oven** (start first): whole chicken at 200°C (~60 min for 1.5kg); tray of vegetables on the lower shelf at the same time
- **Pressure cooker** (start second): a braise of pork belly or a pot of bone broth (40–60 min at high pressure)
- **Induction** (while the above cook): boil eggs; cook a large pot of rice; fry a batch of garlic-ginger oil
- **Prep work**: slice and marinate a batch of proteins for the freezer; quick-pickle some vegetables; hard-boil and peel eggs for a soy sauce marinade

Within 2.5 hours you've produced enough protein, grains, and vegetables for five or six meals, a pot of broth, and a condiment jar you'll use all week.

## A Singapore-Friendly Batch Plan

This is a practical template using ingredients that are widely available, affordable, and work well across multiple dishes.

**Protein 1 — Whole Roast Chicken**:
- Season the night before: rub with salt, a little soy sauce, white pepper, and sesame oil on the skin and inside the cavity
- Roast at 200°C for 60 minutes for a 1.5kg bird (a little longer if larger). Rest before carving.
- Carve and store in portions: breast slices, leg quarters, carcass separately

**Protein 2 — Soy-Braised Eggs (Loh Eggs)**:
- Soft-boil eggs for 6.5 minutes from boiling, then transfer to an ice bath
- Peel once cooled, then simmer in a brine of: 3 tbsp soy sauce, 1 tbsp dark soy sauce, 1 tbsp sugar, 1 cup water, a pinch of five-spice, 1–2 cloves garlic
- Simmer for 10–15 minutes, then let the eggs cool in the liquid. They deepen in colour and flavour over time.
- Keep in the fridge in the braising liquid for up to 5 days

**Grain — Jasmine Rice**:
- Cook 3–4 cups of dried rice (yields 6–8 servings cooked). Use the chicken broth if you have it, or plain water with a pinch of salt and a little sesame oil.
- Store in an airtight container. Use within 2 days — cooked rice carries food safety risks if left too long at room temperature or poorly stored (see Storage section below).

**Vegetables — Roasted + Blanched**:
- Oven: while the chicken roasts, add a tray of broccoli florets, sweet potato cubes, or pumpkin wedges to the lower shelf. Season with oil and salt; they'll be ready in 20–25 minutes at 200°C.
- Induction: blanch a batch of kai lan or kangkong in boiling salted water for 1–2 minutes. Drain and season with oyster sauce and sesame oil. Use within 2 days (leafy greens don't keep as long as roasted vegetables).

**Base Sauce — Garlic-Ginger Oil**:
- Finely slice 1 head of garlic and a large piece of ginger (about the size of your thumb)
- Fry gently in 150–200ml of neutral oil over medium-low heat until the garlic is pale golden and fragrant — 5–8 minutes, watching carefully
- Pour into a clean jar and keep in the fridge
- Use all week: a spoonful in stir-fries, a drizzle over rice, stirred into soups as a last-minute flavour boost

## A Five-Day Meal Plan from One Batch

**Day 1 — Chicken Rice Bowl**: carved chicken breast over rice, with a bowl of broth (made from the carcass simmered with ginger and spring onion), sliced cucumber on the side, a drizzle of the garlic-ginger oil, and chilli sauce if you have it. This is a complete meal in 10 minutes of reheating.

**Day 2 — Chicken Fried Rice**: use the cold, day-old rice from the batch. Cold rice fries better than fresh — the grains are dry and separate rather than clumping. High heat on the induction hob, a little oil, garlic-ginger base, diced leftover chicken, one or two eggs cracked straight in and scrambled through, a splash of soy sauce, and spring onion to finish. 10 minutes.

**Day 3 — Chicken Noodle Soup**: reheat the chicken broth, add egg noodles or rice vermicelli, shredded chicken, a handful of vegetables, and a soy-braised egg halved on top. Season with fish sauce or soy. If you have fresh chilli or chilli oil, that too.

**Day 4 — Soy Egg Rice Bowl**: soy-braised egg, rice, roasted sweet potato or broccoli from the fridge, a drizzle of the braising liquid from the eggs, sesame oil, and whatever sauce you have on hand. If you're cooking a new protein tonight, this is the day to do it — pan-sear a piece of fish or pork chop as the new element while the rest is reheated.

**Day 5 — Fridge Clear-Out Stir-Fry**: whatever is left. High heat, oil, garlic, any remaining vegetables and protein, a generous splash of oyster sauce, over the last of the rice. This is not a formula dish — it's intentional improvisation. Everything in the fridge that is about to expire goes into the wok together. It almost always works.

## Storage Rules

**Cooling before refrigerating**: hot food placed directly in the fridge raises the internal temperature and can affect food safety for everything stored nearby. Cool food to room temperature first — but don't leave it out for more than 2 hours before refrigerating.

**Containers**: airtight containers prevent odour transfer and moisture loss. Reused takeaway containers work; glass containers are better for reheating in the microwave. Label everything with a piece of masking tape: name and date.

**Cooked rice**: this is the item that requires the most care. Bacillus cereus bacteria can survive cooking and multiply in cooked rice left at room temperature. Cool and refrigerate within 1–2 hours. Use cooked rice within 2 days. When reheating, make sure it is steaming hot throughout — not just warm on the outside.

**Cooked meat**: 3–4 days in the fridge for pieces and portions; slightly less for minced or shredded meat.

**Braises, soups, and broth**: 4–5 days in the fridge. They often improve on day 2 and 3 as the flavours continue to develop. Freeze in meal-sized portions for up to 3 months.

## Freezer Strategy

The freezer extends the value of a batch-cooking session significantly. Things that freeze well:

- **Braises and stews**: portion into meal-sized containers, cool completely, freeze. Reheat from frozen in a pot over low heat with a splash of water, or defrost overnight in the fridge.
- **Soups and broths**: freeze in 1-litre or 500ml portions. Labelled ice-cube trays work well for small amounts of stock used as flavour boosts.
- **Cooked beans and lentils**: cook a large pot, portion and freeze. Far better and cheaper than tinned.
- **Raw marinated meat**: marinate pork chops, chicken pieces, or beef slices, portion into zip-lock bags or containers, and freeze in the marinade. Defrost the night before in the fridge — the meat marinates further as it thaws.
- **Bread and baked goods**: freeze in slices, toast directly from frozen.

Things that do not freeze well:
- Cooked rice (texture becomes grainy and separated; not terrible but noticeably worse)
- Fried food (the crust softens and goes soggy on reheating)
- Salads, fresh herbs, and anything you want to eat with its texture intact
- Eggs (cooked or raw; textures change significantly)

Label everything clearly: name, date, number of portions. A freezer you can't read is a freezer you don't use.

## Reheating Properly

Bad reheating undoes good cooking. A few principles:

- **Rice**: add a tablespoon of water before microwaving, cover with a damp paper towel or a plate to trap steam, and heat on medium rather than high. It should take 2–3 minutes and come out moist rather than dried out.
- **Soups and braises**: reheat gently on the stove over medium-low heat, not blasted in the microwave at full power. Add a splash of water or stock if it has thickened too much. Braised meats reheat better when brought back up to temperature in their sauce rather than dry — the liquid prevents the meat from drying out.
- **Vegetables**: roasted vegetables reheat best in a hot oven (5 minutes at 200°C) or in a dry hot pan. The microwave makes them soft and steamy; the oven or pan restores some of the crisp edges.
- **Rice and protein together**: for a quick meal, reheat the rice first, then add your protein and any sauce, heat together for a final minute. They reach eating temperature together and the sauce coats the rice.

## HDB Kitchen Reality

Most HDB kitchens are small — limited counter space, a single sink, and one cooking zone. Batch cooking in this environment requires more organisation, not less.

**Mise en place matters more, not less**: do all washing, peeling, and cutting before any heat goes on. Your counter space is limited; you cannot have half-prepped vegetables and a hot pan at the same time without chaos. Clear the surface before you start cooking.

**Use the sink for cooling**: a pot of boiled eggs or blanched vegetables can cool in the sink in cold running water while you use the stove for the next thing.

**Equipment you need for a good batch session**: one large pot or Dutch oven for the braise and broth, one sheet pan for the oven, and one wok or large frying pan for the induction work. That's three pieces of cookware. You don't need more.

**Stack your cooking**: the oven running at 200°C for the chicken takes about 60 minutes. In that time you can cook rice, boil eggs, fry the garlic-ginger oil, and do any prep for the freezer. You are never just waiting.

## The Hawker Food Release Valve

Batch cooking is not a commitment to cooking every meal at home. Singapore has excellent hawker food at prices that make it a genuine and reasonable option any day of the week.

The goal of batch cooking is not to replace every meal — it's to make sure that on the days when you're tired, time-poor, or just not in the mood, you have something good already prepared. On those days, reheating takes 10 minutes instead of cooking from scratch. On other days, you eat at the hawker centre, and that's not a failure.

A reliable batch cooking habit changes your relationship with the kitchen from something that creates stress — what are we eating tonight? — to something that creates options. Most weeks, that's enough.
