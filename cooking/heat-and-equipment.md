# Heat & Equipment: Induction, Oven and Pressure Cooker

Most home cooks use their equipment on instinct — turn the knob, put the food in, hope for the best. Understanding how your tools actually work changes everything. You stop burning things. You stop wondering why the roast chicken is brown on the outside and raw at the joint. You start getting consistent results.

This guide covers the three pieces of equipment that form the backbone of a Singapore home kitchen: the induction stove, the oven, and the pressure cooker. Each works differently, excels at different tasks, and has failure modes that are completely avoidable once you know what to look for.

---

## Understanding Heat Transfer

Before the specific tools: three ways heat moves from equipment to food.

**Conduction** is direct contact — the pan surface touching the food. A thick cast-iron pan conducts heat more evenly than a thin aluminium pan. When you sear a steak, conduction is doing the work at the contact surface.

**Convection** is heat carried by a moving fluid — either circulating hot air (the oven fan) or hot liquid (a simmering braise). Convection is gentler and more even than direct conduction. It is why a fan-forced oven browns food more evenly than a static one: the circulating air strips away the cool humid boundary layer that forms around food.

**Radiation** is direct energy transfer — infrared from a broiler element or grill. The heat travels in waves and hits the surface of the food without needing a medium. This is why broiling chars and colours the surface quickly while the interior barely warms.

Most real cooking uses more than one. Roasting involves convection (hot circulating air) and radiation (from the hot metal walls of the oven). A braise involves conduction (the pot on the stove) and convection (the liquid moving around the food).

## Induction Stove

### How It Works

An induction hob uses an electromagnetic coil beneath the glass surface to generate a magnetic field. When a compatible pan (steel or cast iron — anything a magnet sticks to) is placed on the hob, the field induces electrical currents in the pan base, which generates heat directly in the metal. The glass surface itself barely heats up. Only the pan gets hot.

This makes induction faster to heat, faster to adjust, and safer than gas. There is no open flame. There is no residual gas. The moment you remove the pan, the heating stops. And the precise power control — especially at the low end — is significantly better than most gas burners, which tend to run hot even at minimum.

If a magnet sticks to the bottom of your pan, it will work on induction. Aluminium and some stainless steel pans (without a magnetic base) will not.

### Searing

Searing is high-heat, dry contact cooking designed to produce the Maillard crust. To do it properly on an induction stove:

- Set the hob to its highest setting and let the pan heat for 2–3 minutes before adding oil
- Pat the protein completely dry with paper towels — surface moisture causes steaming, not browning
- Add oil, let it shimmer or just begin to smoke, then add the protein
- Do not move it. The food will initially stick to the pan; it releases naturally once a crust forms, usually 2–4 minutes for chicken skin, 1–2 minutes for fish
- Do not crowd the pan — if pieces are too close, steam cannot escape and the temperature drops, resulting in grey steamed meat, not a crust

Use stainless steel or cast iron for searing. Non-stick pans cannot safely reach the temperatures needed and will degrade.

**Common mistakes when searing:**
- Starting with insufficient heat — the pan needs to be ripping hot before the food goes in
- Adding food straight from the fridge — cold protein drops the pan temperature dramatically; take it out 20–30 minutes before cooking
- Pressing down on the meat — this squeezes out juice and disrupts the crust formation
- Lifting to check too early — trust the process, wait for natural release

### Sautéing

Medium-high heat, food kept moving. Used for aromatics (garlic, shallots, ginger), quick-cooking vegetables, or finishing cooked proteins in a sauce. The goal is even, controlled colour and softening without burning.

For aromatics: add to oil before the oil reaches maximum heat; you want them sizzling gently and becoming fragrant, not hitting screaming-hot oil that burns them in seconds.

### Simmering

Low and steady. Induction excels here — a low-power setting holds a simmer more precisely than gas, which tends to pulse. For stocks, braises, and porridge, use a low or medium-low setting and cover the pot to retain moisture. If the simmer is too vigorous, uncover slightly rather than dropping the heat all the way.

### Stir-Frying

Use the highest setting. Ideally use a wok with a flat base designed for induction, or a wide stainless steel pan. The critical rule: work in small batches. A home induction stove cannot match the BTU output of a commercial wok burner. If you add too much food at once, the pan temperature drops and you end up braising vegetables in their own steam.

Prep everything in advance (mise en place is non-negotiable for stir-fries), have your sauce measured and ready, cook in batches, and combine everything at the end. A slightly crowded stir-fry made in two batches will always taste better than a single overloaded pan.

### Pan Choice Summary

| Task | Best Pan |
|---|---|
| Searing meat, fish | Stainless steel or cast iron |
| Eggs, fish fillets | Non-stick |
| Stir-frying | Flat-bottom wok or wide stainless steel pan |
| Sautéing, sauces | Stainless steel |
| Soups, braises | Heavy-bottomed pot (stainless or enamelled cast iron) |

## Oven

### Fan (Convection) vs Static

Your oven has two main modes:

- **Fan / convection mode**: a fan circulates hot air around the food. This strips away the humid boundary layer that forms around food, producing faster, more even browning. Use this for roasting vegetables, roasting proteins, sheet pan meals, and anything where you want colour and crispness.
- **Static (no fan)**: heat radiates from elements at top and/or bottom without circulation. Gentler and more humid. Use for delicate baking (cakes, custards, soufflés) where you want even, gentle heat without the drying effect of a fan.

When a recipe gives a temperature, check whether it specifies fan-forced or conventional. If not stated, assume conventional and add 10–20°C if using fan mode, or reduce cooking time by 10–15%.

### Roasting Vegetables

The rules that always apply:

- Preheat oven to 200–220°C (fan)
- Cut vegetables into even pieces — uneven sizes mean some pieces overcook before others are done
- Toss generously in oil, salt, and any spices
- Spread in a single layer with space between pieces — vegetables touching each other steam rather than roast
- Do not crowd the pan; use two pans if needed
- Toss or flip halfway through
- Dense vegetables (root vegetables, pumpkin) take 30–45 minutes; softer ones (zucchini, cherry tomatoes, asparagus) take 15–20 minutes

The most common mistake: a vegetable tray that looks fine but everything is steamed soft and pale. This is caused by crowding, insufficient oil, or too-low heat.

### Roasting Proteins

Different logic from vegetables:

- **Lower temperature (160–180°C fan) for even cooking**: cooking at high heat the whole time produces an overcooked exterior and undercooked interior. Start low to bring the interior to the right temperature evenly.
- **Finish with high heat or broil for colour**: once the internal temperature is close to done, blast with high heat (220°C+ or switch to broil) for 5–10 minutes to brown the exterior.
- **Rest after cooking**: remove from the oven when 5°C below your target temperature. Resting allows carryover cooking and redistributes juices. Cut too early and juice runs out; rest properly and the meat is moist throughout.

### Meat Thermometer — Use One Every Time

Guessing doneness by touch, colour, or time alone leads to inconsistency. An instant-read thermometer costs under $20 and guarantees results.

| Protein | Safe internal temperature |
|---|---|
| Chicken (all cuts) | 75°C |
| Pork | 70°C |
| Beef (medium-rare) | 55°C |
| Beef (medium) | 60°C |
| Beef (medium-well) | 65°C |
| Fish | 60°C |
| Lamb (medium-rare) | 57°C |

Insert the thermometer into the thickest part of the protein, away from bone (bone conducts heat and will give a falsely high reading).

### Sheet Pan Meals

One of the most practical weeknight techniques: arrange protein and vegetables on a single sheet pan and roast together.

The key to making this work is staggered timing:
- Identify the longest-cooking item (typically the protein or a dense root vegetable) and start it first
- Add faster-cooking items partway through
- A chicken thigh might take 35–40 minutes; you might add cherry tomatoes and asparagus in the last 15 minutes

Use a wire rack set inside the sheet pan for the protein if possible — elevating it allows air to circulate underneath and prevents the bottom from steaming in its own juices.

### The Broiler

The broiler (the upper heating element running at maximum) provides intense direct heat from above. It is not for cooking through — it is for surface transformation: caramelising a sugar topping on a baked dish, melting and blistering cheese, charring capsicum skins for peeling, or giving a brown crust to something that has already cooked through.

Watch the broiler constantly. Food goes from perfect to burnt in under two minutes. Keep the oven door slightly ajar if your model allows it, and position the rack no more than 10–15 cm from the element.

## Pressure Cooker (Stovetop or Electric / Instant Pot)

### How It Works

A sealed pressure cooker traps steam, raising the internal pressure and elevating the boiling point of water from 100°C to approximately 120°C. At this higher temperature, food cooks significantly faster — typically 60–70% less time than conventional methods. Tough connective tissue (collagen) that requires hours of low-and-slow braising to break down into gelatin can break down in 45–60 minutes under pressure.

Electric pressure cookers (Instant Pot and similar) add convenience: they maintain pressure automatically and have built-in sauté functions. Stovetop models run at slightly higher pressure and are marginally faster, but require more attention.

### What the Pressure Cooker Excels At

- **Tough cuts**: brisket, oxtail, pork trotters, beef shin, lamb shoulder, pork belly. These cuts have high collagen content that needs to break down for tenderness. A braise that takes 3 hours conventionally takes 45–60 minutes under pressure.
- **Dried beans and legumes**: soaked chickpeas in 15–20 minutes (versus 60–90 on the stovetop); unsoaked black beans in 25–30 minutes.
- **Bone broth and stock**: a stock that takes 4–6 hours on the stovetop is done in 90 minutes under pressure, with a rich, full-bodied result.
- **Congee**: thick, silky rice porridge in 20 minutes versus 45–60 on the stovetop.
- **Root vegetables**: large cuts of sweet potato, daikon, and potato cook through evenly in 5–8 minutes.

### The Workflow

1. **Sauté aromatics first**: this step is often skipped but makes a real difference. Brown onions, garlic, ginger, or your base spices in oil using the sauté function (or in a separate pan). You cannot brown food under pressure — only moist cooking happens there. Any flavour development through browning must happen before the lid goes on.
2. **Deglaze if needed**: if anything is stuck to the bottom after sautéing, add a splash of wine, stock, or water and scrape it up. This prevents the burn warning on electric models.
3. **Add liquid**: pressure cookers require a minimum liquid level to generate steam — typically 250–500ml depending on the model. Check your manual. Do not add so much liquid that you dilute your braise.
4. **Seal and cook**: lock the lid, set to high pressure, and set the timer.
5. **Release pressure**: see below.

### Natural Release vs Quick Release

How you release pressure after cooking matters significantly:

- **Natural pressure release (NPR)**: turn off the heat (or switch to the "keep warm" setting) and let the pressure drop on its own over 10–20 minutes. The food continues to cook gently during this time. Use for: braises, beans, stocks, congee, anything that benefits from gradual cooling and continued cooking. Beans cooked with quick release often split or become grainy.
- **Quick release (QR)**: carefully open the steam valve to vent all pressure at once (use a cloth or tongs — the steam is scalding). Use for: grains (prevent overcooking), vegetables, and anything delicate. If you quick-release a braise, the rapid pressure drop can cause some liquid to be ejected through the valve — not ideal.

For most recipes: use natural release for meats and beans, quick release for vegetables and grains.

### Safety Rules

The pressure cooker is safe when used correctly. These rules are non-negotiable:

- **Never fill more than two-thirds full** — food and liquid expand under pressure. Above two-thirds and the valve can become blocked
- **Check the sealing ring** before every use — a cracked or deformed ring will not seal properly and the cooker will not come to pressure
- **Check the steam valve** is clear and unobstructed
- **Never force open a pressurised cooker** — always confirm all pressure has been released before opening
- When cooking high-starch foods (congee, porridge, apple sauce), use natural release only — the thick liquid can foam and block the valve during quick release

### What the Pressure Cooker Is NOT Good For

- **Delicate proteins** (fish, prawns, tofu): these overcook in minutes even under pressure; use the stovetop or oven
- **Anything that needs a crust**: you cannot brown under pressure; if you want crackling pork belly or chicken with crispy skin, start or finish in the oven or on the stovetop
- **Dairy-heavy dishes**: cream, milk, and soft cheeses curdle under prolonged high heat; add them after pressure cooking is complete, off the heat

## Quick Decision Guide: Which Tool for Which Job

| Food / Task | Best Equipment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Searing a steak or chicken thigh | Induction (cast iron / stainless) | Completely dry protein, high heat |
| Stir-frying vegetables | Induction (wok or wide pan) | Small batches, high heat |
| Sautéing garlic and aromatics | Induction (stainless) | Medium heat, watch closely |
| Soft-boiling eggs | Induction (small pot) | 6.5 min in boiling water |
| Roasting a tray of vegetables | Oven (fan mode, 220°C) | Single layer, toss halfway |
| Cooking a whole chicken | Oven | Low temp first, blast for colour |
| Sheet pan dinner | Oven | Stagger timing for protein vs veg |
| Tough braising cuts (brisket, oxtail) | Pressure cooker | Sauté first, 45–60 min high pressure |
| Dried beans | Pressure cooker | Natural release |
| Bone broth | Pressure cooker | 90 min instead of 4–6 hours |
| Congee | Pressure cooker or induction | 20 min under pressure vs 45 min simmering |
| Delicate fish fillet | Induction (non-stick) or oven | Low-medium heat, watch temperature |
| Melting cheese / browning a top | Oven broiler | Watch constantly |
