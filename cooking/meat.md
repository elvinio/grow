# Handling & Cooking Meat

Meat is the part of a meal most people anchor their confidence to — and also the thing most likely to go wrong. Overcooked chicken breast, tough braised pork that didn't get enough time, a steak that went grey before it developed a crust. Most of these failures come down to a small number of decisions: which cut you're using, whether the surface is dry, whether the heat is right, and whether you rest it after cooking. Get those right and you'll cook meat well consistently.

---

## Buying and Storing Meat

**What to look for at the point of purchase**:

- **Colour**: fresh beef should be bright red; pork should be pale to medium pink; chicken should be pale pink with no grey or green areas; fish should have clear eyes and red gills if whole, or opaque white-pink flesh if filleted
- **Smell**: fresh meat should smell neutral or faintly meaty — clean. Any sour, ammonia-like, or off smell means it's past its best. Trust your nose; it evolved precisely for this.
- **Texture**: meat should feel firm and spring back slightly when pressed. Slimy or tacky surface on chicken or pork is a sign of spoilage.

**Where to buy in Singapore**:
- **Wet markets**: usually fresher than supermarkets because of higher turnover. Buy whole or large pieces — a slab of pork belly, a whole chicken, fish by the piece. The fishmonger will clean and cut to your specification. Cheaper than supermarkets for most cuts.
- **Supermarkets** (NTUC, Cold Storage, Sheng Siong): portioned and labelled, convenient for weeknight shopping. Cold Storage stocks more imported options (Australian beef, New Zealand lamb). Pay attention to the use-by date and choose the freshest available.

**Storage**:
- **Fridge**: use within 1–2 days for chicken and minced meat; 2–3 days for pork and beef cuts. Keep in the coldest part of your fridge (usually the back of the lower shelf, not the door).
- **Freezer**: most cuts keep well for up to 3 months. Beyond that, quality degrades — the food is safe but the texture and flavour suffer. Portion before freezing so you defrost only what you need.
- **Thawing safely**: the safest way is overnight in the fridge. For smaller pieces, cold running water works quickly — seal the meat in a bag and run cold (not warm) water over it. Never thaw on the counter at room temperature; bacteria multiply rapidly in the range of 5–60°C.

## Understanding Cuts: Cheap vs Expensive

The fundamental rule: **tough cuts need slow, low heat; tender cuts need fast, high heat**. Applying the wrong method to the wrong cut is the most common source of disappointing meat.

**Tender cuts** — quick, high heat:
- Chicken breast, pork loin, beef sirloin and tenderloin, fish fillets
- These muscles do little work during the animal's life, so they have little connective tissue. They cook fast and are done in minutes.
- Overcook these and they turn dry, chalky, and unpleasant. They need attention and a thermometer.

**Tough cuts** — slow, low heat:
- Chicken thighs and drumsticks, pork shoulder, pork belly, pork ribs, beef brisket, beef shin, oxtail, lamb shoulder
- These muscles work hard — they're full of collagen and connective tissue. Given enough time and heat, the collagen breaks down into gelatin, transforming the texture from chewy to silky and rich.
- Under-cook these and you get tough, stringy meat. Give them 2–3 hours in a low oven or 40–60 minutes in a pressure cooker, and they become fall-apart tender.
- These cuts are almost always cheaper per kilogram than tender cuts. A pork shoulder braise for four people costs a fraction of four pork tenderloins.

**Singapore-specific availability**: pork belly is widely available in wet markets and supermarkets and is one of the best-value cuts for braising. Whole chickens are easy to find and versatile. Oxtail and beef brisket are at wet markets and Cold Storage; they reward the slow cook.

## Seasoning Meat

**Salt early — dry brining**: the single most impactful thing you can do for most proteins. Season with salt at least 30 minutes before cooking, ideally several hours ahead, and overnight for larger cuts.

What happens: salt draws moisture to the surface, dissolves, and then is reabsorbed into the meat. The result is meat that is seasoned throughout, not just on the outside, and that retains moisture better during cooking. A chicken thigh seasoned 12 hours ahead and one seasoned 5 minutes ahead cook to completely different results, from the same piece of meat.

**Dry rubs**: for roasting and grilling, a rub of salt, pepper, and spices applied ahead of time gives flavour without the mess of a wet marinade.

**Wet marinades**: acid-based marinades (soy sauce, vinegar, citrus, yogurt) work by partially denaturing proteins on the surface of the meat, which can tenderise — but only shallowly, and only with time. A 30-minute marinade adds surface flavour; an overnight marinade adds more depth. However: do not over-marinate in strongly acidic liquids. More than 2–4 hours in a marinade heavy on vinegar or citrus can make the texture of chicken or fish unpleasantly mushy on the outside.

## The Cold-Meat Rule

Take large cuts of meat out of the fridge 15–30 minutes before cooking.

Cold meat placed directly into a hot pan has an uneven temperature gradient — the outside cooks and browns while the inside is still chilled. The result is an overcooked exterior and an undercooked centre. This matters more for thick steaks and large chicken pieces than for thin cuts. For a 2cm-thick steak or a whole chicken leg, this step meaningfully improves how evenly it cooks.

## Pan-Searing on Induction

Pan-searing builds flavour through the Maillard reaction — the browning that happens when proteins and sugars are exposed to high heat. The crust you develop in the first few minutes is where most of the flavour lives.

The method:
1. **Dry the surface**: pat the meat thoroughly dry with paper towels. Any moisture on the surface will steam rather than sear, preventing crust formation. This is non-negotiable.
2. **Heat the pan properly**: set your induction hob to high and let the pan heat for 2 minutes before adding oil. A properly hot pan is essential.
3. **Add oil and heat until shimmering**: a thin, high-smoke-point oil (sunflower, vegetable, or refined coconut) works best. It should shimmer and start to move almost immediately.
4. **Lay the meat away from you**: this protects you from oil splatter.
5. **Do not move it**: the meat will stick to the pan at first and release naturally once a crust forms — usually 2–4 minutes for a chicken piece, 2–3 minutes per side for a 2cm steak. If you try to move it and it resists, it's not ready.
6. **Baste**: once flipped, add a knob of butter, a few garlic cloves, and a sprig of thyme or rosemary if you have them. Tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the meat repeatedly for 60–90 seconds. This adds flavour and helps the top cook through the reflected heat.

A common error: pulling the meat before the crust has formed, then moving it around and preventing it from developing a crust at all.

## Braising (Induction + Oven or Pressure Cooker)

Braising transforms tough, cheap cuts into something deeply satisfying. It's the method behind rendang, tau yew bak (soy-braised pork), and red-cooked chicken — all fundamentally the same process.

The method:
1. **Season and sear**: salt the meat, pat dry, and sear in batches in a heavy pot (Dutch oven or your largest saucepan) until well browned on all sides. Do this in batches — don't crowd. Remove and set aside.
2. **Build the base**: in the same pot, sweat your aromatics in the remaining fat — onion, garlic, ginger, lemongrass, spices, whatever suits the dish. Take your time here; this is the foundation of the flavour.
3. **Add liquid**: pour in stock, water, soy sauce, coconut milk — or a combination. The liquid should come about halfway up the meat, not cover it. Braising is not boiling.
4. **Return the meat**: nestle the seared pieces back into the pot. Bring to a gentle simmer.
5. **Low and slow**:
   - **Oven**: 160°C for 2–3 hours, checking occasionally. The gentle, even heat of an oven gives the most consistent result.
   - **Pressure cooker**: 40–60 minutes at high pressure. Much faster, and the results are nearly as good — the texture is slightly different (pressure cooking is more aggressive) but the flavour is rich and the meat is tender.
6. **The sauce**: the braising liquid at the end is already a sauce. For a richer result, remove the meat and reduce the liquid over high heat on the induction hob until it thickens. Taste and adjust seasoning.

## Resting Meat

Every cooking guide mentions resting meat, and people still skip it. Don't skip it.

When meat is on the heat, the muscle fibres contract and push juices towards the centre of the piece. Cutting immediately releases those juices — they run across the board and you eat drier meat. Resting gives the muscle fibres time to relax and reabsorb the juices.

**How long to rest**:
- Large roasts (whole chicken, pork shoulder, beef joint): 15–20 minutes, loosely tented with foil — not wrapped tight, which would steam and soften the crust
- Steaks and chicken pieces: 5 minutes on a warm plate

A simple way to remember it: the larger the cut, the longer the rest.

## Food Safety

These are not optional niceties. Foodborne illness from undercooked meat is serious.

**Safe internal temperatures** (use a probe thermometer — visual judgment is unreliable):
- Chicken: 75°C at the thickest part, away from bone
- Pork: 70°C (pink pork is safe if it's reached this temperature — do not judge by colour alone)
- Beef: 55–57°C for medium-rare; 60–63°C for medium; 70°C+ for well done
- Fish: 60°C, or when the flesh flakes and is opaque throughout

**Cross-contamination**:
- Keep raw and cooked meat completely separate — different boards, different knives, different areas of the counter
- Wash hands, boards, and knives with hot soapy water after handling raw meat
- Never put cooked meat back on a plate or board that held raw meat

**The colour rule is unreliable**: pork can be pale pink and perfectly safe at 70°C; chicken can be grey-looking but still undercooked near the bone. Use a thermometer.

## Singapore-Specific Tips

**Pork belly**: before braising, blanch the whole slab briefly — 5 minutes in boiling water, then rinse. This removes blood, fat impurities, and off-flavours from the surface, and gives the finished braise a cleaner taste. It's standard practice in Chinese cooking and makes a real difference.

**Whole chicken**: one of the most efficient things you can cook. Season generously the night before — salt the skin and cavity. Roast at 200°C for about 60 minutes for a 1.5kg bird (longer for larger birds). Let it rest, then carve into portions. The carcass makes broth.

**Eggs as daily protein**: eggs are cheap, versatile, and reliable. Soft-boil in bulk (6.5 minutes from boiling, then ice bath, then keep in shell in the fridge for up to 5 days). Use in rice bowls, ramen, salads, or as a standalone snack. A batch of soy sauce marinated eggs (loh eggs) — simmered in soy sauce, water, sugar, and five-spice for 10 minutes — keeps in the fridge for a week and goes with almost everything.

The goal with meat is not perfection every time. It's understanding why things go right or wrong, so you can repeat the good results. Dry the surface. Season early. Use the right heat for the right cut. Rest before cutting. These four habits will take you a long way.
