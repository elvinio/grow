# Cooking Grains: Rice, Oats, Pasta, Noodles and More

Grains are the most reliable, forgiving, and economical part of any meal plan. They batch cook easily, store well, reheat without fuss, and form the base of almost every cuisine in the world. Once you know the ratios and timing for the grains you eat regularly, you never need to look them up again.

This guide covers the grains that matter most in a Singapore home kitchen — starting with rice, which earns its own extended section — plus the oats, pasta, noodles, and other grains worth adding to your rotation.

---

## Why Grains Matter

Grains are:

- **The easiest ingredient to batch cook**: a large pot of rice or quinoa at the start of the week provides the foundation for multiple meals with minimal daily effort
- **Highly forgiving**: unlike proteins, which overcook quickly, most grains have a wide window where they are edible to excellent
- **Economical**: a kilogram of jasmine rice costs very little and provides carbohydrate for an entire week
- **Versatile**: cooked rice becomes fried rice, congee, rice bowls, or a side dish; cooked pasta becomes a salad, a warm toss, or a baked dish

The main skill with grains is learning the ratios — water to grain — and the timing for each type. Get those right and grains cook themselves.

## Rice: The Centrepiece of the Singapore Home Kitchen

Singapore runs on rice. It appears at almost every meal in some form — steamed alongside a stir-fry, in a bowl of congee for breakfast, as the base for chicken rice, or leftover and fried the next morning. Understanding rice well gives you more return than almost any other cooking skill here.

### Types of Rice and Their Characters

**Jasmine rice** (Thai fragrant rice)
The everyday rice of Southeast Asia. Long-grain, slightly sticky when cooked, fragrant with a faint floral aroma. Holds its texture well. Best for pairing with curries, stir-fries, and most Southeast Asian dishes.

**Short-grain Japanese rice** (Japonica)
Stickier and slightly sweeter than jasmine. The right choice for Japanese-style rice bowls (donburi), sushi, and any dish where you want rice that clumps together slightly. Often sold as "sushi rice" in Singapore.

**Basmati rice**
Long, slender grains that remain fluffy and separate when cooked. Aromatic, with a nutty character. Pairs with Indian curries and biryanis. Needs more rinsing and ideally a 30-minute soak before cooking.

**Brown rice**
The whole grain version of white rice, with the bran layer intact. Nuttier, chewier, higher in fibre. Requires significantly more water and time — about 40–45 minutes on the stovetop versus 15 for white rice. Worth knowing: mixing brown and white rice 50/50 is a practical middle ground for nutrition without dramatically changing texture.

### Rinsing Rice

Rinse all rice before cooking. The surface carries loose starch that makes cooked rice gluey rather than fluffy. Rinse under cold water in a bowl or colander, gently agitating with your hand, until the water runs mostly clear — usually 3–4 rinses. Do not rinse vigorously; you want to remove surface starch, not break the grains.

Exception: some Japanese-style recipes and risottos deliberately keep the starch for a creamier result. For everything else, rinse.

### Cooking Methods

**Rice cooker (recommended for daily use)**

The rice cooker is one of the best kitchen appliances in a Southeast Asian home. Set and forget; it switches to warm automatically and holds without overcooking.

- Rinse the rice first
- Water ratio for jasmine rice: 1 cup rice to 1.2 cups water (slightly less water than a 1:1.5 ratio because the rice cooker is a sealed environment that traps steam)
- Short-grain Japanese rice: same 1:1.2 ratio or follow the markings on the inner pot
- Brown rice: 1:1.5 to 1:1.75 — the rice cooker often has a dedicated brown rice setting
- Do not open the lid during cooking; do not skip the rest period (5–10 minutes on "warm") before serving

**Stovetop absorption method**

No rice cooker? This method works just as well with attention.

1. Rinse the rice, then combine with water (1:1.5 ratio for jasmine) in a heavy-bottomed pot
2. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, uncovered
3. Once boiling, stir once, then reduce heat to the absolute lowest setting and cover tightly
4. Cook for 15 minutes — do not lift the lid during this time
5. Remove from heat and rest, still covered, for 5–10 minutes
6. Fluff gently with a fork or rice paddle before serving

The most common mistake: lifting the lid. Every time you open the pot, you release steam that the rice needs to finish cooking. Trust the method.

**Pressure cooker**

Fast and reliable for large batches.

- Rinsed rice + 1:1 water ratio (the sealed environment is very efficient)
- 3 minutes on high pressure
- 10 minutes natural release — do not quick release or the rice blows open and turns mushy

### Fried Rice: The Golden Rule

Use day-old cold rice. This single rule is the difference between excellent fried rice and a clumped, gluey pan of starch.

Fresh rice is too moist and the grains stick together; when you add them to a hot pan they clump and steam rather than fry. Cold refrigerated rice has dried out slightly, and the grains separate easily.

If you must use fresh rice: spread it on a tray, fan it, and refrigerate uncovered for at least 30–60 minutes before frying. It is not as good as overnight but it works in a pinch.

Other fried rice principles:
- Very high heat, small batches (no more than 2–3 cups rice per batch)
- Season with light soy sauce and a dash of dark soy for colour
- Add eggs into an empty section of the pan and scramble before mixing with rice
- Sesame oil and white pepper go in at the very end, off the heat

### Congee

Congee is rice cooked with an enormous amount of water until the grains break down into a thick, silky porridge. It is one of the most comforting and practical things you can make — good for breakfast, illness, or any time you want something warm and restorative with minimal effort.

**Basic ratios:**
- 1 cup jasmine rice to 8–10 cups water or stock (more water = thinner congee; less = thicker)
- Add sliced ginger to the pot for warmth and aroma

**Stovetop method**: bring to a boil, then reduce to a low simmer and cook 45–60 minutes, stirring occasionally to prevent sticking. The longer it cooks, the silkier it becomes.

**Pressure cooker method**: 1 cup rice to 8 cups water, 20 minutes high pressure, natural release. The result is indistinguishable from an hour of stovetop simmering.

**Seasoning and toppings**: season the congee itself with salt and a splash of light soy sauce. Finish individual bowls with sesame oil, white pepper, and your choice of toppings — century egg and salted pork, a soft-poached egg, julienned ginger, spring onions, fried shallots, or simply good soy sauce and chilli oil.

## Oats

Oats are one of the fastest, most nutritious, and most adaptable breakfast grains. They are also chronically underseasoned in most home kitchens.

### Rolled Oats (Stovetop)

Rolled oats (also called old-fashioned oats) are oat groats that have been steamed and flattened. They cook in about 5 minutes and produce a creamy, substantial porridge.

- **Ratio**: 1 part oats to 2 parts liquid (water, milk, or a mix)
- Bring the liquid to a simmer, add oats, cook on medium heat for 4–5 minutes, stirring occasionally
- **Season with salt first** — a pinch of salt in the oats as they cook transforms them from cardboard to flavourful. Do not skip this.
- Sweeten afterwards: honey, maple syrup, or brown sugar; add toppings (banana, fresh fruit, nut butter, seeds) after the heat is off

Quick oats (instant oats) are pre-cooked and cut finer — they take 1–2 minutes but have a mushier texture. Fine for convenience, but rolled oats are worth the extra few minutes.

### Overnight Oats

No cooking required. Combine in a jar or container the night before and refrigerate.

Standard ratio:
- 1/2 cup rolled oats
- 1/2 cup milk (dairy or plant-based)
- 1/4 cup plain yoghurt (optional but adds creaminess and protein)
- 1 teaspoon chia seeds (optional — adds body and nutrition)
- Pinch of salt

Stir, cover, and refrigerate overnight. In the morning, add toppings: fresh fruit, granola, nut butter, honey. Keeps for up to 3 days in the fridge, making it ideal for batch prep.

### Savoury Oats

An underrated preparation worth trying: treat oats like congee. Cook rolled oats in stock (chicken or vegetable) instead of water, season with soy sauce and white pepper, and top with a soft-boiled egg, spring onions, and a drizzle of chilli oil. The result is warm, filling, and genuinely satisfying — much more interesting than sweet oatmeal and surprisingly quick.

## Pasta and Noodles

### Dried Pasta

The rules that produce good pasta every time:

**Use a large pot with abundantly salted water.** The water should taste distinctly salty — not ocean-level, but the kind of water you would not want to drink. This is how pasta gets seasoned from the inside. A pot of completely unsalted water produces flat-tasting pasta that no sauce can fully rescue.

**Cook 1–2 minutes less than the packet says.** Pasta continues cooking after you drain it, especially when you toss it in a hot pan with sauce. The packet timing assumes you are eating immediately from the pot; in practice, finishing pasta in the sauce (standard Italian technique) means you need it slightly underdone when it comes out of the water.

**Reserve a cup of pasta water before draining.** This is not optional if you are making a pan sauce. Pasta water is starchy and seasoned — it emulsifies with oil and fat to create a silky, cohesive sauce. Without it, oil-based sauces break and slide off the pasta. With it, even a simple garlic-oil sauce becomes glossy and unified.

**Finish in the pan.** Transfer drained pasta into the sauce in the pan over medium heat, add a splash of pasta water, and toss. The starch in the water binds everything together. This extra 60–90 seconds in the pan makes a noticeable difference.

### Dry Egg Noodles, Bee Hoon (Rice Vermicelli), and Glass Noodles

These behave differently from Italian pasta and benefit from a different approach.

- **Dry egg noodles** (the kind used in wonton noodle soup or dry noodles): boil briefly, 2–3 minutes, until just tender. Do not overcook or they become mushy. Rinse under cold water after draining to stop cooking and prevent clumping.
- **Bee hoon (rice vermicelli)**: for stir-frying, soak in cold water for 15–20 minutes until softened, then drain and add directly to the wok. This produces better texture than boiling. For soups, cook briefly in the broth itself.
- **Glass noodles (tang hoon, mung bean vermicelli)**: soak in boiling water for 5–8 minutes until soft and translucent. They absorb sauce aggressively, so add them near the end of cooking and work quickly.

### Instant Noodles, Elevated

Instant noodles are a legitimate weeknight option when treated as a base rather than a finished dish. Use the flavouring packet sparingly (most are heavily sodium-loaded), and add:

- A soft-boiled or poached egg (add to simmering water for 6.5 minutes for a jammy yolk)
- A handful of leafy greens (spinach, baby bok choy, or whatever is in the fridge — the hot broth wilts them)
- A drizzle of sesame oil and chilli oil at the end
- Spring onions or fried shallots if you have them

Total upgrade time: 5 extra minutes. The result tastes meaningfully better and is more nutritionally complete.

## Other Grains Worth Knowing

### Quinoa

Technically a seed, but used and cooked like a grain. Complete protein with all essential amino acids — useful if you are cooking for someone reducing meat intake.

**Critical step: rinse thoroughly.** Quinoa is coated in saponins, a natural bitter compound that causes digestive discomfort if not removed. Rinse under cold water in a fine mesh strainer for at least 60 seconds.

- **Ratio**: 1 cup quinoa to 1.75 cups water
- Bring to a boil, reduce to a simmer, cover, cook for 15 minutes
- Rest off heat for 5 minutes, then fluff with a fork
- It is done when the germ (a tiny spiral) has uncoiled from each grain

Treat cooked quinoa like rice for batch cooking — it keeps well for 4 days refrigerated and works equally well warm or cold in salads.

### Pearl Barley

A satisfying, slightly chewy grain with a mild, nutty flavour. Excellent in soups and stews where it absorbs flavour from the liquid around it, and in grain salads dressed with vinaigrette.

- **Stovetop**: 1 cup barley to 3 cups water or stock, simmer for 30–40 minutes
- **Pressure cooker**: 1 cup barley to 2.5 cups liquid, 10 minutes high pressure, natural release
- Pearl barley (the most common variety) has the outer husk removed, so it cooks relatively quickly. Hulled barley retains more of the bran layer and takes longer.

Add pearl barley directly to soups and stews — it will absorb liquid as it cooks, thickening the broth naturally. Be aware that it continues absorbing liquid in storage, so add more stock when reheating.

## Storing Cooked Grains

**Cool completely before refrigerating.** Putting hot grains in a sealed container traps condensation, making them soggy and accelerating bacterial growth.

| Grain | Fridge | Freezer |
|---|---|---|
| Cooked rice | Up to 2 days | Up to 3 months |
| Cooked pasta | 3–4 days (tossed in a little oil) | Not recommended — texture deteriorates |
| Cooked oats | 3–4 days | 2–3 months |
| Cooked quinoa | 4–5 days | Up to 3 months |
| Cooked barley | 3–4 days | Up to 3 months |

**Rice safety note**: cooked rice carries a specific risk from *Bacillus cereus*, a bacterium that produces heat-stable toxins and thrives when cooked rice is left at room temperature for more than 2 hours. Do not leave cooked rice sitting on the counter. Refrigerate within an hour of cooking. When reheating, make sure the rice is steaming hot all the way through — not just warm. The 2-day maximum for refrigerated rice is not conservative; it is a genuine food safety guideline.

**Freezing rice and grains**: spread cooked grains on a tray to cool, then freeze in individual portions (roughly 1 cup each). When reheating from frozen, a splash of water and a microwave at medium power for 2–3 minutes (covered) produces fluffy, fresh-tasting rice. Alternatively, thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat in a covered pan with a splash of water on low heat.

Batch-cooked, portioned, and frozen grains are one of the highest-leverage habits for a busy week. They eliminate the decision fatigue of "what starch goes with this?" and reduce weeknight cooking time significantly.
