# Cooking Vegetables: Selecting, Prepping and Getting the Most Flavour

Vegetables are the most commonly under-delivered part of a home-cooked meal. They come out pale, soft, and bland — and then people conclude they don't like vegetables. The problem is almost never the vegetable. It's the cooking. Three things are almost always missing: enough heat, enough fat, and enough salt. Fix those three, and even the most ordinary kangkong becomes something you want to eat.

---

## Why Vegetables Disappoint

Most home cooks make the same cluster of errors:

- **Too little heat.** A crowded pan on low heat doesn't roast vegetables — it steams and stews them. You get grey-green mush instead of golden caramelised edges.
- **Too little fat.** Fat carries flavour, promotes browning, and makes vegetables feel satisfying rather than sparse. Being stingy with oil is one of the fastest ways to make food taste like diet food.
- **Too little salt.** Salt doesn't just season — it draws out moisture, concentrates flavour, and makes everything taste more like itself. Vegetables salted only at the very end taste flat. Season during cooking, not just at the finish.
- **Skipping the dry step.** Washing is essential, but wet vegetables hitting a hot pan just steam. Pat them dry before cooking.
- **Crowding the pan.** Add too many vegetables at once and the temperature drops. Steam builds. Everything goes soft. You need room for moisture to escape.

These are not nuanced errors. They're mechanical, and fixing them costs nothing.

## Selecting Vegetables in Singapore

You have two main options: the wet market or the supermarket. Both have their place.

**Wet markets** (Tekka Market in Little India, Tiong Bahru Market, your nearest neighbourhood market) offer the freshest produce, usually sourced locally or from nearby Malaysia and Indonesia. Prices are lower, especially for everyday greens. The vegetables turn over fast. Get there before 10am for the best selection.

What's usually excellent value and reliably fresh at wet markets:
- **Leafy greens**: kangkong (water spinach), kai lan (Chinese broccoli), xiao bai cai (Shanghai bok choy), chye sim (choy sum), sweet potato leaves
- **Long vegetables**: long beans, lady's finger (okra), brinjal (eggplant)
- **Roots and gourds**: bitter gourd, daikon, sweet potato, taro
- **Aromatics**: garlic (local and imported), ginger, shallots — buy in bulk and keep in a cool, dry spot

**Supermarkets** (NTUC FairPrice, Cold Storage, Sheng Siong) are more convenient, especially during weeknight shopping. They carry consistent local staples alongside a wider range of imported produce. They're also your source for things the wet market rarely stocks: broccoli, cauliflower, cherry tomatoes, zucchini, capsicum, asparagus.

**When to use imported produce**: for roasting or when you need a specific texture or flavour that local alternatives don't offer. Imported broccoli, cauliflower, and cherry tomatoes roast beautifully. For stir-fries and everyday cooking, local greens are fresher, cheaper, and usually better suited to the techniques.

**Reading freshness**:
- Leafy greens should look lively — deep green, no yellowing, no slimy patches
- Brinjal should feel firm and heavy; avoid those with soft spots or wrinkled skin
- Long beans should snap cleanly when bent, not bend limply
- Bitter gourd should feel firm; bumpy skin is normal

## Prepping Vegetables Properly

**Washing leafy greens**: Fill your sink or a large bowl with cold water, submerge the greens, and agitate gently. Grit and dirt fall to the bottom. Lift the greens out (don't pour — that just pours the dirt back over them), drain the water, and repeat at least once more. Kangkong, kai lan, and xiao bai cai particularly need this — they're often sandy.

**Drying**: After washing, pat greens dry with a clean cloth or kitchen paper, or use a salad spinner if you have one. This step matters more than most cooks realise. Water on the surface of vegetables in a hot pan generates immediate steam, preventing the browning and caramelisation you're trying to achieve.

**Cutting to consistent size**: Uneven cuts mean uneven cooking. The thick stem piece burns before the thin leaf piece cooks. For broccoli or cauliflower going into the oven, aim for florets of similar size. For stir-fried greens, cut or separate stems from leaves — the stems go in first because they need more time.

**Mise en place**: This is the French phrase for having everything ready before cooking starts. In practical terms: wash, cut, and prepare all your vegetables before you turn on the heat. Once the wok is hot, things move fast. You don't have time to be chopping garlic when the oil is already smoking.

## Cooking Methods and When to Use Each

### Stir-Frying (Induction, High Heat)

The fastest method and the right one for leafy greens. Total cooking time: 2–4 minutes.

The sequence:
1. Heat the wok or pan on the highest induction setting until it's genuinely hot — a drop of water should evaporate immediately on contact
2. Add a generous amount of oil (2–3 tbsp for a wok) and let it heat until shimmering
3. Add garlic and/or ginger, stir constantly for 20–30 seconds until fragrant — don't walk away, it burns fast
4. Add your greens and toss constantly with tongs or a wok spatula
5. If the stems need more cooking: add a small splash of water or stock (2–3 tbsp), let it steam briefly with the lid on for 30–60 seconds
6. Season at the end: a pinch of salt, a splash of soy sauce, a drizzle of oyster sauce — whatever suits the dish
7. Off the heat, add sesame oil if using, and plate immediately

What works well: kangkong, kai lan, xiao bai cai, chye sim, sweet potato leaves. Basically any leafy green that would go sad and yellow if you left it even a minute too long.

### Roasting (Oven, 200–220°C)

Best for dense vegetables that can take high, dry heat. This method brings out sweetness through caramelisation and gives you slightly charred edges that add depth.

The method:
- Cut vegetables into even pieces — broccoli into similar-sized florets, sweet potato and pumpkin into 2–3cm cubes, brinjal into thick slices or cubes
- Toss with enough oil to lightly coat (don't drown them, but don't be stingy)
- Season with salt before going into the oven
- Spread in a single layer on a sheet pan — do not overlap, do not crowd (use two pans if needed)
- Roast at 200–220°C, tossing or flipping halfway through
- Vegetables are done when the edges are golden, caramelised, and slightly charred

Time guide: broccoli 15–20 min, cauliflower 20–25 min, sweet potato 25–30 min, brinjal 20–25 min, pumpkin 30–35 min.

### Blanching Then Saucing

A simple method that works beautifully for broccoli, bok choy, and long beans. You get bright colour, tender-crisp texture, and then dress the vegetables with a sauce.

The method:
- Bring a pot of well-salted water to a rolling boil (it should taste faintly like the sea)
- Blanch the vegetables: 1 minute for leafy bok choy, 2–3 minutes for broccoli or long beans
- Remove and either drain or transfer to a serving plate immediately
- Dress while still hot: the simplest version is oyster sauce drizzled over the top, finished with a little sesame oil

### Braising

For vegetables that can take slow, gentle cooking in liquid — daikon, lotus root, taro, tofu. Often done alongside meat, where the vegetables absorb the flavour of the braising liquid. The liquid should come about a third of the way up. Low heat, long time. Daikon braised in a pork or chicken broth, with soy sauce and a little sugar, becomes something surprisingly satisfying.

### Raw and Quick-Pickled

A quick pickle adds brightness, acidity, and crunch to heavier meals. It takes 10 minutes to prepare and is ready in 30 minutes.

Basic ratio: 1 cup water + 1 cup rice vinegar + 1 tsp salt + 1–2 tsp sugar. Bring to a boil, dissolve the salt and sugar, pour over thinly sliced vegetables, cool, and refrigerate. Cucumber, carrot, daikon, and red onion all work well. A small pile of these alongside a braised pork dish or a rice bowl cuts through the richness and lifts the whole plate.

## Seasoning Vegetables Well

The most common seasoning error is waiting until the food is on the plate. By then, the salt sits on the surface rather than working into the food.

**Season during cooking**: when the vegetables go into the pan, season them. When you add liquid, season it. Taste as you go and adjust.

**Fat is flavour**: the purpose of oil in stir-frying isn't just to prevent sticking. Fat carries and amplifies flavour. Use enough to coat the vegetables.

**The garlic-ginger base**: for almost any Asian vegetable dish, start with finely minced garlic and/or ginger fried in oil until fragrant. This takes 20–30 seconds. Do not skip it, and do not rush it — under-fried garlic tastes raw, burnt garlic tastes bitter.

**Finishing moves**: once off the heat, small additions make a large difference:
- A drizzle of sesame oil
- A squeeze of lime or a splash of black vinegar
- A light coating of oyster sauce
- A pinch of sugar to balance bitterness (works well with kai lan and bitter gourd)

## Avoiding Common Mistakes

- **Overcrowding the pan**: if you're cooking a large batch, do it in two rounds, or accept that the first batch will be less good. A crowded pan steams, not sears.
- **Low heat for stir-fry**: stir-frying requires genuinely high heat. If your pan doesn't get properly hot, the food will be soggy. On induction, use the highest setting and give the pan time to actually heat up before you add oil.
- **Skipping the dry step**: after washing, dry the vegetables. Wet food + hot pan = steam.
- **Under-seasoning**: taste before plating. Adjust. If it needs more salt, add more salt.
- **Overcooking leafy greens**: 2–4 minutes in a hot wok is usually enough. When in doubt, take them off the heat earlier rather than later. They continue cooking in residual heat.

Good vegetable cooking is mostly about removing obstacles: wet surfaces, low heat, crowded pans, and too little seasoning. Remove those obstacles and the vegetables do most of the work themselves.
